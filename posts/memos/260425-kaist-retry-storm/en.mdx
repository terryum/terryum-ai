> Where the previous post [The Quiet Bill — When Vercel Bills You for Bots](/en/posts/260419-vercel-quiet-bill) covered "why the invoice ballooned (the structural problem)," this one covers "what exactly happened that day, and what was my responsibility (the postmortem of one incident)."

## TL;DR

- April 9–12: a single KAIST IP sent **about 50 million edge requests** to three broken images on a single survey site — a retry storm.
- The trigger was **two faults compounded**: (1) figure paths still pointing at the old per-chapter folders while files had moved to the new flat layout (404 on every figure), (2) the `<img onerror="this.src='{src}'">` pattern re-firing forever in headless environments.
- External automation (an LLM agent's browsing tool or a headless crawler) looped on top of those two faults for four days.
- I learned about it from the invoice — **the biggest problem was that I didn't even notice while the incident was still happening.**
- Mitigation: one line of code (`this.onerror=null`), a figure-path validator, migration to Cloudflare, an Access-OTP rule to identify that IP if it returns, and billing/anomaly alerts.
- KAIST stranger, if you're reading this — get in touch. You owe me a meal.

## What happened

Vercel invoices were normally in the single-digit dollars. One day in April, an abnormal charge showed up. I asked for an analysis, and the answer came back:

> "I've taken a look at the traffic from April 8th → April 12th and do indeed see the spike you are referring to. After digging into the traffic, it appears that these requests originate from Korea Advanced Institute of Science and Technology and the majority of the requests are to image URLs. All these requests occur from the same IP address with the same referrer URL `https://survey-robot-hand-tactile-sensor.vercel.app/ko/ch10.html`."

Single IP, consistent UA, same referrer. Not a distributed attack — **one person (or one automation tool) hammering the same three images on the same page** for four days. Vercel's diagnosis was also "not DDoS."

Average ~150 requests per second. Roughly 17 million per image. **Numbers no human or normal browser could produce.**

## Why it ran away — the product of two faults

### Fault 1: figure paths pointing at files that no longer existed

When the survey was a standalone, figures lived under per-chapter subfolders like `assets/figures/ch10/fig_10_X_<slug>.png`. Just before merging into the monorepo, the standard switched to flat naming (`ch10_<slug>_fig<N>.png`). Files moved, but **a standalone build that still carried the old `ch10.html` paths went out as-is.** Result: every image request 404'd.

### Fault 2: an `onerror` self-loop

One line in `shared/build_site.py:479`:

```python
onerror="this.src='{src}'"
```

The intent was to fall back to the light-mode image when the dark-mode one was missing. But **when both dark and light were broken**, headless environments kept firing the `onerror` over and over. Normal browsers self-guard against re-firing on the same `src`; headless Chromium and some LLM-agent browser tools have weak or no such guard.

### External automation on top of both faults

Someone at KAIST likely fed that chapter (embodiment retargeting) to an automation tool — something like "summarize this page" or "download all the figures here." The tool opened the page headlessly and tried to fetch the images, all 404. The retry policy ran without a termination condition. The natural ending around April 12 was probably a closed laptop or a session timeout.

**No malice apparent.** A textbook case of automation getting stuck and not knowing it was stuck. But that person's ignorance (or bad luck) shows up directly on my invoice — that's the cost structure of this new era.

## My fault

That the bill got to an abnormal amount before I noticed wasn't bad luck — it was an absence of design. Four points.

### 1. I didn't finish the migration cleanly

I moved the files when switching to flat naming, but **I didn't check whether deploys with the old paths were still live.** There was no build-step rule that asks "do all the figures this page references actually exist on disk?" If there had been, the April 8 deploy would never have shipped.

### 2. I didn't put a guard on `onerror`

`onerror="this.src='{src}'"` isn't fail-safe even when the intent is right. **The one line that nulls `onerror` on first failure was missing.** I tested only in normal browsers and never verified the behavior in a headless environment.

### 3. There was no automatic cap on cost

Vercel keeps billing without limit once a payment method is on file. **Spend limit, billing alert, daily-threshold alert — none of them set up.** Putting a credit card on a Pro plan without automating "I trust this much, no further" is just being defenseless.

### 4. Monitoring was effectively nonexistent

Not a single email arrived through several days of traffic anomaly. **The invoice was the alert.** The billing cycle is monthly, so that's a signal as much as 30 days late. Not having a channel where I could see the anomaly myself is barely distinguishable from not monitoring at all.

If even one of these four had been in place, the incident would never have started, or would have been cut within an hour of starting.

## What I've done since

Stacked layers. So that if one layer is bypassed, the next one catches.

### Code

- **`shared/build_site.py:479` — one-shot `onerror`**: `this.onerror=null;this.src='{src}'`. Stops re-firing on the first failure. Caps requests per image at two.
- **Stronger figure validation in `shared/validate.py`** — build fails on the appearance of an `assets/figures/ch{N}/` subfolder, plus a file-existence check for every figure referenced in the body.
- **GitHub Actions PR validate** — the rules above run automatically at PR stage. Broken paths can't reach `main`.

### Infrastructure (Vercel → Cloudflare migration + Cloudflare protection)

- **Migrated to Cloudflare Pages** — unmetered egress. A recurrence of the same incident yields zero invoice change.
- **WAF Rate Limiting Rule** — the same IP hitting `/assets/` more than 100 times in 10 seconds is blocked.
- **Bot Fight Mode** — known bad-bot UAs are auto-challenged.
- **AI Bot Block + AI Labyrinth** — GPTBot/ClaudeBot/PerplexityBot and other LLM crawlers blocked automatically; bots that try to evade get sent into a fake maze that burns their resources. **The most direct answer to the possibility that this incident was an LLM agent.**
- **Browser Integrity Check + Email Obfuscation + automatic Polyfill replacement** — general hygiene.
- **Security.txt + robots.txt content signals** — explicit opt-out from AI training.
- **DDoS L7 alert + Billing $10 alert** — email, not the invoice, becomes the first signal.

## So what changed

If the same incident hits tomorrow:
- **Zero financial loss** — Cloudflare egress is free.
- **Traffic itself blocked within a minute** — Rate Limit + Bot Fight Mode + AI Bot Block, any one layer catches.
- **Immediate awareness** — the DDoS L7 alert lands in email.
- **Recurrence itself is hard** — broken figure paths are blocked at PR stage; the `onerror` self-loop is gone from the code.

The change isn't "stronger defense" but **the existence of any defense at all**. Before, there was literally none.

## Lesson — even static sites can run away on cost

For a long time I believed static site = safe cost. HTML/CSS/JS as a bundle, no server code. A wrong assumption.

- On a host that bills static assets by **edge request count**, one bot hitting 50 million times costs you 50 million times.
- Automation tools (especially LLM-agent browsing tools) commonly retry **without a termination condition** — intent-free.
- "Static site, therefore safe" needs to become "where is the egress meter?"
- That Cloudflare offers free unmetered egress isn't just a pricing policy — it's **structural insurance against this kind of incident.**

## The bigger picture

The real message of this incident isn't to blame someone at KAIST. It's that **in an era where automation is universal, intent-free incidents become routine.** Machine ignorance (or a badly designed retry policy) generates more cost, more often, than human mistakes do.

So every public asset has to start from four baseline assumptions: **infrastructure that cuts at the meter + automation-friendly fail-safes + automatic alerts + automatic billing caps.** This incident was an expensive tuition.
