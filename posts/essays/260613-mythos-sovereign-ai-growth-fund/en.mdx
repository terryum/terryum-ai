The export ban on Anthropic's Mythos 5 and Fable 5 shows that AI is no longer just a software product. The U.S. government reportedly sought to restrict these models not only from foreign governments and companies, but also from foreign nationals inside the United States, and Anthropic said it would cut off access for all customers in order to comply. How large the technical risk actually was is still unclear. What matters is that access to frontier models is moving toward the same coordinates as electricity, ports, and semiconductor equipment: strategic assets controlled by states.

Robotics is already showing a similar scene. In the Iran war, drones and unmanned systems are no longer cheap auxiliary weapons. They have become a way for states to shake shipping routes, air defenses, and industrial facilities. Machines that are cheaper than manned aircraft, politically easier to lose, and deployable at scale are changing the surface of war. If a country depends entirely on American or Chinese companies for both the brain and body of its robots, then the moment those companies or governments shut off access, national security can collapse in an instant.

In the future, AI and robots will be used as closely as electricity or the internet. Work, life, medicine, education, defense. If they are cut off, the national shock may be worse than a blackout. The deeper AI and robots penetrate the nervous system of a state, the more vulnerable a country becomes when it is borrowing someone else's nervous system.

In that sense, Korea's decision to build independent foundation models is not wrong. Sovereign AI means having at least a minimal brain that does not shut down in a crisis. Sovereign AI for national infrastructure and security cannot be solved by every company building its own small model. Data, talent, GPUs, evaluation, and verification are all games of scale. So the government is trying to help Korean "national champion" companies invest at a scale comparable to big tech. That may indeed be the path for Korea to remain a sovereign state between the United States and China.

But just because the large direction is right does not mean the government and startup ecosystem are moving in the right way. My concern is growing. The national direction is right. We need sovereign AI, sovereign robots, and perhaps sovereign quantum computers and sovereign AI scientists. But I worry that we are treating the conclusion too lightly when it is translated directly into "pour huge capital into a few companies."

In AI and robotics, concentration of resources may be unavoidable. To compete with Google, Meta, and OpenAI in the United States, or Baidu, Huawei, and Unitree in China, Korea also needs national support that brings in world-class talent and concentrates resources like GPUs and data. Unlike the old voucher-style government AI budgets that were distributed "fairly" but weakly, I broadly agree that Korea may need to accept criticism of favoritism and concentrate resources if it wants to create a Korean Anthropic or a Korean Unitree.

But the dream of having a Korean Anthropic will not be solved simply by pushing hundreds of billions of won into a small number of companies. If "backing the number-one company" becomes the success formula, the goal of startups and VCs shifts from "are we building technology customers actually want?" to "how quickly can we look like number one?" Some companies raise enormous capital only a few years after founding, obtain national-champion status, and then move toward a too-big-to-fail path with additional investment from the National Growth Fund. People in the field may still ask, "Is this really usable?" but once national security enters the sentence, verification weakens and VCs can secure a much more certain return. It is a bitter sight.

I felt a similar discomfort around the recent controversy over a company claiming to build an AI scientist. I do not want to name a specific company here. But when leading VCs invested hundreds of billions of won, did they really believe the claim that this company had built something superior to GPT? Perhaps belief was not the main point. Perhaps the more important thing was a story that could attract the next round of capital. If a company becomes the first and largest in a category, money follows, government endorsement follows, time is granted, and the technology can later be made true by hiring the right people. That may have been enough to justify the investment.

VCs are supposed to be risk capital. They should take risk alongside startups attempting something uncertain. But after seeing a few national-champion startups succeed, some VCs now seem more focused on which company will become a candidate for the Korea Development Bank or the National Growth Fund than on which company satisfies customers. If media play and capital play can create a hero worth hundreds of billions of won, who will keep obsessing over truth, technology, small revenue, and customer satisfaction? Where did real startup investing go?

This does not mean the state should withdraw. It means the opposite. The more money the state puts in, the more precise its questions must become. If Korea wants independent models, it should ask which layer must actually be controlled: model weights, data, inference infrastructure, safety evaluation, or deployment rights for defense and public systems. If Korea wants humanoids, it should look past the valuation of a finished-product company and ask where the bottleneck is: actuators, hands, batteries, simulators, manufacturing data, or field deployment. If the National Growth Fund is truly a growth fund, candidate companies must be able to fail out when they do not meet performance, customer, revenue, and security standards. A national strategy should be a map of bottlenecks, not a name tag.

Sovereign AI is necessary. Sovereign robots may be necessary too, and perhaps sovereign quantum computers and sovereign AI scientists. But the word sovereign should make us more rigorous, not more lenient. It is not a spell for saving a chosen company in the name of the state. It is the work of building national operating capacity that does not shut down in a crisis. Sovereignty is not created by raising a few giant trees. Only when another forest can grow after one giant falls can a country become a sovereign state with technology that does not tremble before someone else's switch.
