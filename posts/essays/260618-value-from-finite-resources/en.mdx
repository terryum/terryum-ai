When ChatGPT came out and I first used Deep Research, I felt that the productivity of text had risen sharply. With a few lines of instruction, a broad research report appeared, and I thought, "Maybe we no longer need consulting reports from famous firms." I felt the same thing when I first used Claude Code. With a few lines of instruction, it used multiple tools to plan, build, and debug software, and I thought, "Maybe we no longer need junior developers."

That is true. Productivity did rise. But if I can move and produce 10 times faster, can I create 10 times more market value? If I can write ten books in the time it once took to write one, or build ten pieces of software in the time it once took to build one, will I really earn 10 times more money?

For perhaps a year of transition, outsourced development may capture some of the margin from this productivity gain. But that window will not last long. As more people use the same tools and more teams say, "We build with AI for cheaper," prices will fall. Soon we may produce 10 times as much while unit prices fall to one-tenth, leaving value right where it started.

As I wrote in [the shift in where money is made after the calculator](/en/posts/260522-ai-agent-outcome-operations), calculators quickly drove the price of calculation toward zero, and the business of "we calculate for you" disappeared. Outsourced development should not celebrate too quickly either. Instead, the calculator's domain expanded into vertical outcomes such as accounting software and production ERP. In other words, making calculation 10 times faster did not create 10 times more money. To earn the same money, one had to take responsibility for a larger domain.

> "AI is the new electricity" (Andrew Ng, 2017)
> "Intelligence is a utility like electricity or water" (Sam Altman, 2026)

AI tokens are now electricity, and software is becoming a kind of appliance that can be made even in developing countries. As I wrote in [Showing Off Because You Use AI](/en/posts/260514-ai-superpower-time-lag), using AI is closer to plugging into electricity as a basic condition of participation. It cannot become the fundamental competitiveness of a person or a company. And as I argued in [Software Becomes Cosmetics](/en/posts/260425-sw-becomes-cosmetics), just as cosmetics became something anyone could make through ODMs like Cosmax and the market turned into a brutal marketing fight, in a market where anyone can make software, selling will matter more than making.

Put differently, when a fan can replace a person waving a hand fan for 24 hours, we do not pay 100 times more for a fan whose performance is 100 times better. Eventually we pay something close to the electricity bill. In the AI era, many innovative pieces of software made by burning tokens will appear. But most software not based on truly precious technology or rare data will eventually be paid close to the token cost.

Then where does value remain? What will people actually pay for? I believe market value is still placed on bottlenecks created by finite resources, and the core of those bottlenecks is still human.

The first bottleneck is human time. If ten pieces of software can be made in the time one used to take, the market will soon treat ten as the new baseline. There was a time when compiling code took punch cards and a day of waiting. Now a build can run with a click, but no one pays dozens of times more for that click. People do not price only the quantity of output. The effort required to make it and the scarcity required to obtain it are also part of the price. If a piece of software does not make clear which problem it chooses and which outcome it takes responsibility for, its value is token cost, no more and no less.

The second bottleneck is relationships. The first relationship is the relationship with customers. Making will matter less than selling, and customers also have finite time. They will not compare everything. They will buy through existing trust relationships. That may be a brand, a relationship with a favored influencer or celebrity, or in B2B, a sales network and trust built over a long time.

Relationships on the research and development side also matter. Starting alone and building alone is now something anyone can do. That means it is hard to price highly. By contrast, in a world where there are many ways to enjoy things alone, many ways to work alone, and where being alone can feel easier than being with others, the ability to make many people gather and work together becomes rare. The power to attract great talent, to try again with colleagues who have shared hardship, and to connect across companies, universities, industries, governments, and global networks is far more valuable than software made by one person plugging into an electrical outlet.

What kind of person will you become? I recently visited a startup class at Seoul National University, and I felt a little disappointed that many students had imagined businesses where they sat in front of a computer and commanded AI agents. The reason is simple. I can do that too. Even if I did not know how, I could click and copy it. Precisely now, we need to run farther outside the software world: have diverse offline experiences, suffer and fail in different ways, and cultivate the trust of colleagues and customers gained along the way as a precious asset.

I do not think I have outstanding engineering ability. My research ability is a little better, but it is not comparable to truly great scholars. My ability as a startup CEO was lacking, and as an executive in a large organization it is even more lacking. But I have walked a path, and along that path I have accumulated encouragement and trust. These days I am starting many things with professors at Seoul National University, and I am always grateful to the professors and colleagues who first suggest that we work together. I hope I can continue to do great work with great people.
