When AI tools suddenly give you a superpower, it is easy to feel far ahead of the people who do not have it yet. Work that took hours yesterday now finishes in minutes, and everyone else looks as if they are still wandering around without knowing the shortcut.

Claude Code feels like that right now. People who can use it a little talk about harness engineering, Ralph loops, context management, and look down a bit on people who still cannot use it well. Of course productivity goes up. For a while, the gap may be real. But that gap does not prove the depth of the person.

I remember the first time I used Deep Research last year. It was shockingly good. Before that, if I wanted to understand something, I had to browse the web, find English articles and reports, translate them, and read them myself. Deep Research took one question, searched broadly, and turned it into a report with sources. It felt like I had gained a superpower for quickly understanding a new field.

At the time, I thought Deep Research would help me grow quickly and lift the company's productivity dramatically. People who were not using it looked frustratingly slow. So I kept saying things like: we should adopt Google across the company, outside research can be solved quickly with Deep Research, NotebookLM should become a productivity tool. If AI tools could make me several times faster, I thought the company should change just as quickly.

Then the vice chairman asked me this:
"If it is that good, competitors will eventually use it too. Can it really become our weapon?"

At first, it sounded like a sentence that blocked change. I had tried Deep Research myself and was impatient to spread it across the company. I thought people who had not used it were underestimating its power. But with time, I realized he was right. We did not adopt it for a year, and nothing happened to the company. If I had pushed the change when people did not have a strong need for it, it probably would have created confusion and resistance. And whether we adopted it or not, our revenue probably would not have changed much.

If I had gone around giving seminars on Deep Research as if I had discovered a new civilization, I would now feel rather embarrassed. That does not mean it would have been wrong. But my depth should have gone beyond that. If the highest point of the knowledge structure I had built was merely delivering the news that Google had launched Deep Research, that would have revealed how shallow I was. As a manager, I should at least have asked the more fundamental question: can this really become our weapon?

Of course someone has to try tools early. Someone has to introduce them to the organization, help colleagues, and turn them into workflows that fit real work. But there is no need to get smug about it. "I use this so well." "You still don't know this?" "I understand the future." None of that becomes a company's fundamental value. In the short term, we may collect useful wins: work we once paid agencies for can be done internally, or we can build our own software at a much lower cost. But every company will experience that. It is not our weapon.

What AI replaces is mostly the surface of work. Finding materials, making sentences, writing code, organizing drafts. What remains with people who have thought for a long time is different: what to ask, which answer makes sense, why an experiment is likely to fail, and how to grow a person in the right direction. In the end, a person's value comes from what they have built over time. The same is true for organizations. Adopting AI early does not suddenly create strategy, customer understanding, execution, culture, or trust. And being clumsy with the newest tool does not erase someone's research depth or judgment.

On the other hand, it is hard to build depth from a life spent learning every new click before others, selling it as a lecture, and then anxiously chasing the next Big Tech announcement. That life may look smart for a while. It knows new tool names, new workflows, and words other people have not heard yet. But that alone is hard to sustain. Today's superpower becomes tomorrow's common feature.

It is important to use AI well. It is also important to learn quickly. But we should not connect a tool we learned slightly earlier to our own superiority. Being good at Excel matters, but failing to use a new Excel feature does not lower a person's value. And making everyone use a new Excel feature does not suddenly raise a company's competitiveness. We should reallocate time and effort, but we should not hand everything over to AI and just click. Fundamental value and depth come from time that has been properly built.

There is no need to rush. There is no need to get smug. In an age where knowledge flies around like shorts, it feels more important to define my real work clearly, focus on it, and identify the right tool for solving my actual problem instead of falling into the stream. AI can greatly amplify what I have built, but it cannot live the time I never spent.
