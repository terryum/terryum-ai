I think a slump has arrived. This is an awkward period of waiting for something to begin, so the number of things I can throw myself into and execute hard has gone down. As execution became ambiguous, my mind sank with it.

Oddly, I think I was healthier a month ago, when I was burning tokens without stopping. At first it was exciting, then it became addictive. Still, I would wake up, hurry to the computer, burn through tokens, wait for the reset, and then burn with energy again. Life felt more willing then. But as I wrote in [the essay that value does not come from burning tokens](/en/posts/260618-value-from-finite-resources), I recently began thinking about the meaning of burning tokens indiscriminately and making software. Then I somehow fell into the swamp of government projects, then the swamp of hiring, and before I knew it I had become someone who did not even burn many tokens anymore.

Why did I fall into a slump? After thinking about it, I realized it was because I was trying to find "meaningful work" before I acted. Once I decided that burning tokens had no meaning, I stopped burning tokens. And because there are limits to "meaningful preparation" until the robot operating environment is ready in a month or two, I could no longer identify good actions that would produce results. Then action stopped, my mind stopped, and I fell into the slump. Whether in a startup or a new business, one must execute because the result is unknowable and learn through the process. I seem to have fallen into the opposite dilemma: because I cannot know the result, I cannot clearly execute anything.

Anne-Laure Le Cunff's [Tiny Experiments](https://youtu.be/sxxnqc1mu08?si=A1sgErr-WIx3Flu_) explains this shift of perspective well. Many people assume the linear model is correct: set a goal, make a plan, work hard, achieve it, and then become happy. But the more uncertain the future is, and the more ambiguous the present is, as in startups, research, career transitions, or new businesses, the less this model works. If the goal itself is uncertain, everything that follows becomes confused. It is an easy model to sink into the same swamp I am in now.

The method of Tiny Experiment is simple.

> "I will do this action for this duration."

It is not as grand as a goal, and it does not demand lifelong persistence like a habit. You take a small action for a fixed period, then read the result not as success or failure but as data. Until now, I was asking control questions: "How can I succeed?", "Am I on the right path?", "What kind of person am I originally?" Tiny Experiments suggests replacing them with experiment questions: "What can I try?", "What will this attempt tell me?", "What kind of self will this attempt reveal?" It also suggests following internal indicators such as "Do I gain energy from this work?" and "Does it become clearer as I repeat it?", rather than only outcome-driven external indicators.

The reason my action slowed and I fell into a slump is probably that I was focused on "the result of the robot moving." Government projects may be delayed, and robot delivery may be delayed. But if that makes me say, "I just want this time to pass quickly," and slows down my actions too, I am pushing myself into a slump. Instead, I need to do what I can each day, discover one thing about myself and the work through it, and raise my energy level. The belief that burning tokens equals productivity was wrong, but the cynicism that burning tokens is meaningless also seems wrong. What matters is daily execution and discovery.

Motivation is not only the cause of action. It is also the result of action. A slump is less a state where motivation has disappeared than a state where the line between action and result has blurred. Rather than finding motivation from results, I need to grow it by acting and discovering the internal indicators that change through those actions. Self-efficacy does not suddenly appear after a large success. It forms as the feeling of having completed small actions repeats.

Today I woke up at 4 a.m. and ran in the mountains. Then I came to work early and wrote the essay I had promised myself I would write every day. Even if they are not big things, I will pack today with things to do, execute them, and raise my own motivation.
