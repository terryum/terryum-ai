There is a wound from high school that has stayed with me for a long time. I ran for student council president, beat six competing teams, and won. Then the election was annulled. The reason was that I had greeted students arriving at school early in the morning, which violated the election rules. Objectively, it was my mistake. It was a failure born of ignorance and excessive zeal.

The real wound came after that. During the month between the election and its annulment, rumors spread through the school that I had handed out bribes or bought votes. The other candidates and their supporters formed a single front, and I quickly became the public enemy of the whole school. Through repeated committees and delegate votes, I watched close friends turn away and strangers look at me with accusation. At that age, it left a wound that was hard to bear.

At that time, the story of Zhuge Ke helped me look back on myself. Zhuge Ke was the son of Zhuge Jin, an important minister of Eastern Wu, and the nephew of Zhuge Liang. He was brilliant from youth and grew up with Sun Quan's favor. After Lu Xun's death he became General-in-Chief and held political power. His father, Zhuge Jin, worried that his son's habit of displaying his talent too openly would one day ruin the family. That worry later came true.

After taking power, Zhuge Ke pushed ahead with a large northern campaign despite opposition. He ultimately lost, and what was worse was that he shifted the blame for the defeat onto others. Having already lost public support through arrogance and repeated misrule, he was killed in Sun Jun's coup after a single defeat, despite many earlier victories, and his family line was cut off. Zhuge Jin's fear had proved right.

For some reason, that story made me think of my high school years. I, too, was pushed into the abyss by a single error of judgment. At first I thought it was because of opposing supporters and rumors. Looking back, however, perhaps I also received no next chance because, like Zhuge Ke, I lacked virtue. I wanted to look smart, to get ahead, to win. When I shone, people applauded, envied me, and wanted to be my friend. But when defeat arrived, the hostile voices that had been hidden began to rise. Like Zhuge Ke, I lacked virtue and met a bitter end after a single defeat.

At the same time, when public figures suddenly fall, I think it often reveals the hostility that had been growing behind their applause. Still, in our time, it is difficult to blame such falls only on a personal lack of virtue. As the saying goes, the protruding stone gets struck. Behind fame, envy and resentment often condense.

In the East, the qualities of a leader are often described as intelligence, virtue, and physical strength. In the language of the Three Kingdoms, this is close to the strategist, the warrior, and the virtuous commander. One reads the board with the mind. One breaks through the battlefield with the body and will. One wins people's hearts and creates a greater force through them. In the past, intelligence and physical strength were great assets for leaders. Those who knew more, judged faster, endured longer, and pushed harder moved ahead. But in the age of AI, the price of those two axes is falling quickly. Knowledge is augmented by models, and much of execution is taken up by AI and robots.

What remains, then? I think virtue becomes heavier. By virtue, I do not mean a reputation for being nice. I mean the power to recognize people, grow them, not discard them easily in crisis, not take their credit, and still keep the standard clear. Warm but not soft. Strict but not cold. People do not move only for benefits such as high pay. When memories and actions accumulate of someone seeing and supporting me warmly over a long period, a virtuous person responds with devotion beyond calculation. That is the force of a virtuous commander.

There are people who care for me without an obvious reason. Some may wonder why they treat me so generously. But because there is no obvious reason, it is natural that the care is hard to explain. Virtue does not always appear like an invoice. Some people see possibility first, give opportunities to those who have not yet proved themselves, and trust a long direction more than a small result. I learn what virtue is from such people. I often think of Chairman Kyungsoo Lee of Cosmax in this way. To win someone's heart is not to give that person a benefit. It is to make part of that person's life feel connected to your purpose.

I am still a person lacking virtue. Even now, I create friction at work, make internal enemies, and sometimes earn resentment through unrefined words and actions. When my competence and execution also falter, there is little reason for anyone to trust and support such a person. But I do have the effort to become a virtuous commander. Even if my nature was not born that way, even if when I was young I received affection and resentment like Zhuge Ke, I want to grow older on a path where I serve and work with people better than myself. I want to grow a vessel worthy of standing beside them.

When I write a piece like this, I first feel the debt of grace I have not yet repaid, and the need to apologize to those I hurt with a narrow heart. I do not know whether I am truly suited to leadership, or whether I will live as a north star for many people. Still, as I grow older, I want to exchange more affection and encouragement with others. If I had to give that path a name, I would call it the path of the virtuous commander.
