There is a wound from high school that has stayed with me for a long time. I ran for student council president, beat six competing teams, and won. Then the election was annulled. The reason was that I had greeted students arriving at school early in the morning, which violated the election rules. Objectively, it was my mistake. It was a failure born of ignorance and excessive zeal.

The real wound came after that. During the month between the election and its annulment, rumors spread through the school that I had handed out bribes or bought votes. The other candidates and their supporters formed a single front, and I quickly became the public enemy of the whole school. Through repeated committees and delegate votes, I watched close friends turn away and strangers look at me with accusation. At that age, it was hard to bear.

At first I remembered the event only as an injustice. I had broken a rule, but I had not bribed anyone or bought votes. Over time, however, the question changed. Why did one mistake bring me down so easily? Why did so many people move so quickly toward condemnation instead of defense?

The story that helped me see that question again was the story of Zhuge Ke. Zhuge Ke was the son of Zhuge Jin, an important minister of Eastern Wu, and the nephew of Zhuge Liang. From an early age he was known for his talent and received Sun Quan's favor. His pacification of the Shanyue and recruitment of soldiers were real achievements, and after Lu Xun's death he took charge of military affairs as General-in-Chief. After Sun Quan died, he became Grand Tutor and regent for the young Sun Liang. Talent, family background, and power gathered around one person very quickly.

But his father, Zhuge Jin, is said to have worried about him. His concern was that a son who displayed his talent too openly, carried too much confidence, and treated people lightly would one day endanger the family. In fact, after his victory at Dongxing, Zhuge Ke pushed ahead with a large northern campaign despite opposition. He attacked the heavily defended fortress of Xincheng at Hefei and refused to withdraw easily through a long siege, heat, and disease. He eventually failed. Worse was what followed. Instead of repairing morale and public trust, he suppressed opponents and prepared another campaign. Soon afterward he was killed in Sun Jun's coup, and his family suffered with him.

For some reason, that story made me think of my high school years. A student election and a Three Kingdoms power struggle cannot be placed on the same scale. But they resemble each other in one respect: a person's failure is not judged only by the single mistake in front of everyone. Some mistakes are sentenced by the shape of the relationships built before them. A person who has accumulated virtue receives a chance to explain. A person who has accumulated resentment gives others the excuse they were waiting for.

I had thought that one error of judgment had pushed me into the abyss. At first I blamed the opposing supporters and the rumors. But looking back, perhaps I did not receive a second chance because I had not earned enough trust. I wanted to look smart, to get ahead, to win. When I shone, people applauded, envied me, and wanted to be my friend. But when defeat arrived, the hidden hostility surfaced. Perhaps the applause had not been trust in me as a person. Perhaps it was only light reflected off the surface of victory.

Of course, not every fall can be explained by a lack of virtue. In our time, fame almost always gathers envy, fatigue, and anger behind it. People look for flaws in those who shine. So when someone falls, we cannot simply conclude that the person lacked virtue. But in my own case, I do not want to avoid the question. Did I really win people's hearts? Or did I only carry them for a while on the momentum of victory?

In the East, the qualities of a leader are often described as intelligence, virtue, and physical strength. In the language of the Three Kingdoms, this is close to the strategist, the warrior, and the virtuous commander. One reads the board with the mind. One breaks through the battlefield with the body and will. One wins people's hearts and creates a greater force through them. In the past, intelligence and physical strength were great assets for leaders. Those who knew more, judged faster, endured longer, and pushed harder moved ahead. But in the age of AI, the price of those two axes is falling. Knowledge is augmented by models, and much of execution is taken up by AI and robots.

What remains, then? I think virtue becomes heavier. By virtue, I do not mean a reputation for being nice. I mean the power to recognize people, grow them, not discard them easily in crisis, not take their credit, and still keep the standard clear. Warm but not soft. Strict but not cold. People do not move only for high pay or good conditions. When memories accumulate of someone seeing me warmly for a long time, believing in my possibility, and not abandoning me immediately after failure, a feeling beyond calculation begins to form. That is the force of a virtuous commander.

There are people who care for me without an obvious reason. Some may wonder why they treat me so generously. But because there is no obvious reason, it is natural that the care is hard to explain. Virtue does not always appear like an invoice. Some people see possibility first, give opportunities to those who have not yet proved themselves, and trust a long direction more than a small result. I learn what virtue is from such people. I often think of Chairman Kyungsoo Lee of Cosmax in this way. To win someone's heart is not to give that person a benefit. It is to make part of that person's life feel connected to your purpose.

AI transformation touches the same problem. Technology can be installed, but hearts cannot. When AI and robots enter a factory, the field quietly asks: does this technology help me or push me out? Does it recognize my know-how or take it away? If it fails, will I be blamed, and if it succeeds, will someone above me take the credit? A field that does not trust its leader closes its mouth. When the field closes its mouth, tacit knowledge does not rise. The veteran worker's hands, smell, viscosity, machine sounds, seasonal variation, and subtle differences between material lots move through relationships, not documents.

That is why the virtuous commander of the AI era cannot merely be a good person. An incompetent virtuous commander collapses an organization into sentimentalism, and goodwill without standards exhausts good people. The future virtuous commander must not be someone who gives up intelligence and strength, but someone who builds trust on top of intelligence and strength expanded by AI and automation. Someone who uses smarter tools, builds faster execution systems, and still makes people feel safe enough to bring out their real ability. Someone who shares credit, does not evade responsibility, cares for people, and does not lower the standard of performance.

I am still a person lacking virtue. Even now, I create friction at work, make internal enemies, and sometimes earn resentment through unrefined words and actions. When my competence and execution also falter, even I find it difficult to answer why anyone should trust and support me. But at least I can see the direction I want to go. Even if my nature was not born that way, even if I was once someone like Zhuge Ke who received affection and resentment at the same time, I want to grow older on a road where I serve and work with people better than myself. I want to grow a vessel worthy of standing beside them.

When I write a piece like this, I first feel the debt of grace I have not yet repaid. I also feel the need to apologize to those I hurt with a narrow heart. I do not know whether I am truly suited to leadership, or whether I will live as a north star for many people. Still, as I grow older, I want to exchange more affection and encouragement with others. If I had to give that path a name, I would call it the path of the virtuous commander.
