Leverage is the multiplier on what one person's one hour of work produces. If you're a leader, you naturally had to spend your time on high-leverage work. And the method was singular: grow an organization and move people. So no matter how brilliant a developer you were, the end of every career was becoming a manager — and those who led bigger organizations and converted that into results wielded greater influence.

But the formula has changed. In the AI agent era, the cost of replicating yourself has begun converging to zero.

Define a few markdown files well, and AI agents that resemble me research, write, code, and even conduct studies. In the past, you'd hire a junior, spend six months training them, and barely get one person's worth of output. Now, build one good harness and dozens of 'me' work in parallel. You don't need to assemble an organization with others — you can create leverage by replicating yourself.

So where is the bottleneck in this era? It's me.

Sure, even with limited ability, I could build AI agents smarter than myself. But the one directing them, connecting their output to real-world results — that's still me. The leader of AI agents being excellent matters enormously (for now, at least). This is the same reason a startup founder must be exceptional. A CEO can work with employees more skilled than they are, but the startup's ceiling converges to the founder's ceiling. AI agents are no different. The resolution of the problems I define, the height of the standards I set, the eye with which I judge quality — these become the ceiling for the entire agent fleet.

So this year, I decided to go back to being a player.

Until the year before last, as a startup CEO, I took on everything — fundraising, hiring, financial management — all the "rest" so my teammates could focus as 'players.' Last year as a corporate executive was the same. Aligning direction between C-suite and teams, breaking down data silos across organizations — a political role. I rarely touched code or conducted AI research myself. That was leverage-1 work, a zone no startup CEO or corporate executive could afford to settle into.

But this year is different. With the cost of replicating me approaching zero, the highest leverage is pushing the quality of the original — 'me' — to its extreme. AI agent output is bounded by my level. So what I must do is singular: raise my own ceiling. Only then does the quality of my replicas rise, and only then does the system generate leverage efficiently. Even now, in my Physical AI research focused on robot hands, I'm working to maintain balance between what I read myself and what AI agents read for me.

In this era, my most important resources are neither money nor people. They are my time, stamina, focus, and the ability to tune out noise. Not being swayed by trivial distractions. Not spending time on work that isn't real work. Not building what doesn't need building. And on the other hand, reading properly and writing properly, by my own hand. That's why I [decided to read and write by hand, every day](/posts/260406-writing-daily-by-hand).

No matter how powerful a weapon Claude Code is in my hands, I won't spend all my time swinging it wildly. I won't chase tool developments — harness engineering, multi-agents — and drown in social media feeds. Because excellence always comes from within. This is still a time when human excellence matters.
