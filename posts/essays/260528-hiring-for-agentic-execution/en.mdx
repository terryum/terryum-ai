As a startup CEO, I have hired close to a hundred people. I have interviewed several hundred more in the process. Startup hiring often begins with a coffee chat, where both sides try to understand each other more deeply. In those conversations, I often think of the three criteria Sam Altman described in his 2014 Y Combinator lecture [How to start a startup](https://youtu.be/CVfnkM44Urs?si=NLtmUJeUuOjO9I6d&t=899).

- Intelligence: "Are they smart?"
- Execution: "Do they get things done?"
- Character: "Do I want to spend a lot of time around them?"

The first condition of talent is competence. A large part of that comes from intelligence. Someone who works hard can become good, but when a smart person works hard for a long time, the result is usually stronger. That is the kind of person we need to find.

Second, a competent person cannot merely think and talk. People with execution often already have an answer when the CEO asks, "What if we tried this?" They have thought about it, tested it, and reached a conclusion. The opposite kind of person gives ten thousand reasons why it cannot be done. Good people just do it.

Third is character. If I had to choose between a highly skilled cofounder and a person I can trust as a human being, I would choose the latter. Skill can be bought with money, but trust and attachment do not appear in an instant. When I meet juniors, I also try to see through deep conversation whether this person's grain fits mine. It is similar to why someone cannot become a partner, however attractive they are, if being next to them feels uncomfortable. People need to feel at ease with each other to communicate honestly and stay together for a long time.

But in the AI era, these criteria need to change. AI already provides forms of intelligence and execution that are stronger than humans in some dimensions. No matter how much knowledge a human tries to keep in their head, and no matter how relentlessly they try to execute, they cannot simply match the raw intelligence and execution of AI. Then what kind of talent does this era require?

Let me first share a few ideas I agree with.

Some people say only entrepreneurs will remain. In the AI era, if you have the will to do something, AI can handle much of the execution. What remains is setting the mission and vision, defining the problem you want to solve, and pushing through it with obsession and persistence. A person with those qualities is, in one word, an entrepreneur.

Others say we need to become owners. In the AI era, the work is less about doing everything myself and more about assigning work to many AI agents, setting their direction, reviewing what they return, giving the next instruction, and integrating the output of the whole agent team. In the analog world, the people who did this were owners. Said differently, employees who only do what they are told will find it harder to survive.

These ideas point toward the solopreneur. We now live in an ironic era where companies need to hire owner-like people who do not need to be employed, and investors need to back founder-like people who do not need investment. That makes hiring and investing harder. It also makes it harder for juniors to get hired or funded, because the bar has already moved toward people who would succeed anyway.

So I am thinking about this a lot. There must still be gems among juniors. What kind of person should we select, and with what eyes? How should Sam Altman's three questions be redefined for the AI era?

I do not think intelligence, execution, and character disappear as the three axes. A company is still a place where people think, execute, and pursue a shared purpose with others. But I do think we need to add a fourth axis: entrepreneurial ownership. The meaning of intelligence, execution, and character also needs to be adjusted.

- Entrepreneurial ownership: "Can they turn ambiguity into business outcomes?"

Entrepreneurial ownership is the power to turn a problem into a business outcome. The more we work with AI agents, the harder it becomes to connect output to business results unless the person operating them holds the center. They need to know why the work matters, where the finish line is, and who receives what value. Only when they close that loop does the work move forward. Good talent must be employee-like and founder-like at the same time, and founder-like while still knowing how to use the resources inside an organization.

Intelligence still matters. But the value of intelligence that stores a lot of knowledge and says plausible things in a meeting is decreasing. Intelligence in the AI era is closer to judgment. It is the ability to break an ambiguous problem into parts, identify the core constraint, and distinguish which answers can be trusted from which ones must be verified directly. This kind of intelligence cannot be judged by school names or credentials. We need to see what hard problem they defined, how they solved it, and how they measured and verified that it had actually been solved.

Execution becomes heavier. If someone says they move less because AI agents exist, they are probably not strong talent. They should be busier, because they are running many agents at once, reading the results, catching what is wrong, giving the next instruction, and leaving behind something that actually works. The important question is not how many times their personal productivity increased. It is whether that productivity reached the company's final metrics: revenue, users, retention, or cost structure. Execution that overwhelms in both quantity and quality, in the right direction, is what separates talent.

Finally, character is what allows a solopreneur to become part of a real company. The question is still close to "Do I want to spend a lot of time around them?" What changes is what kind of person I want to spend that time with.

In the past, because people worked mostly with other people, a good colleague was someone whose ego was not too strong, who cooperated well, and who resolved conflict smoothly. But in an AI native company, each teammate is closer to a small company employing several agents. So the person I want to work with for a long time is not merely a pleasant person. It is someone who can operate the small company inside themselves maturely, bring promised results, and align them with the company's goals without creating heavy communication costs.

That is why I think a good AI native company will become closer to a sports team full of superstars. Each person must create overwhelming results in their own position. But the fact that everyone is a superstar does not make the team win. They need to train together, read each other's movements, endure difficulty, and connect themselves to one result: the team title. Character is what prevents the company from becoming a team of rude superstars. Individuals matter, but this is still a game where the team has to win.

Because the bar has risen this high, hiring is becoming more difficult. Companies complain that they need to hire founder-like people, but those people do not want to join companies. Investors complain that they need to invest in companies where a small number of superstars can create large impact, but those companies do not need capital. We are entering an era where only people who do not need employment can get employed, and only companies that do not need investment can get investment.

There is no shortcut except to pass through the difficult path until reaching that level. Until then, no one will help. You may join a small startup, accept less money, and receive more opportunities. You may go beyond assigned research at school and produce company-level outcomes. You may start a company yourself and grow with life-or-death pressure. But only after passing through that path, and becoming someone who no longer needs employment, do you become the talent everyone wants.

So for juniors, I hope you do not over-optimize for small conditions. If a good opportunity appears from the perspective of growth, go there and build experience. Conversely, even if you are already at a good large company, if that place is stagnant from the perspective of growth, move to a place with a ladder. Today's salary does not guarantee tomorrow's career. The eye for talent is rising, and as AI agents improve, human labor below that level will be absorbed quickly. Do not look at yourself through the eyes of a corporate recruitment process. Raise your own eyes.

"Are they smart?"
"Do they get things done?"
"Do I want to spend a lot of time around them?"
"Can they turn ambiguity into business outcomes?"
