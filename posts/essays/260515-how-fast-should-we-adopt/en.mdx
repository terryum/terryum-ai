Whenever GPT or Claude releases a new feature, people immediately publish explainers, usage notes, and workflows. Open social media and the feed is full of why this feature matters, why it must be learned now, and why not knowing it means falling behind. Watching that stream makes me feel as if I should try it right away. When I have not even clicked it, and have nothing to add to the conversation, I start to feel behind the times. I do not actually catch up; I only accumulate the tired feeling that I should. Is that okay?

I think that fatigue is legitimate. It is not that I am wrong. The world is tiring, and the instinct to step away from that fatigue is a reasonable response.

Of course, some people do need to learn first. If your work is teaching the latest trends, running a technology YouTube channel, or translating new tools into language others can understand, then speed is part of the job. When a new feature appears, you need to try it, organize it, and sometimes even exaggerate a little so that people can grasp what changed. That kind of content is useful. Someone has to taste the change first and mark it on the map.

But not everyone has that job. For most people, the important question is not "Do I know this?" but "Do I need to know this now?" If learning it today rather than later does not meaningfully change the core metrics of my work, then chasing every piece of knowledge is not competence. It is cost. The reason is simple: learning later often costs less. A feature that requires complex settings and unfamiliar concepts today may enter the default UI six months from now, come with much better tutorials and examples, or disappear so completely that it never needed to be learned at all. The late learner does not pay the trial-and-error cost of the first wave.

Think about GPTs at the end of 2023. ChatGPT was still spreading everywhere, and people talked as if the next app store had opened. Everyone seemed ready to build their own AI chatbot, publish GPT apps, and create a new distribution channel on top of it. Google's agent-builder-style tools felt similar. The promise that anyone could easily make an agent had real impact. But if we ask whether that had to be learned right then, the answer is fairly plain. Some people still use it well, but for most people it was not knowledge whose absence would break their life or work.

This does not mean every change can be accepted late. The important distinction is between features and directions. Features change their names every week and appear and disappear. Directions change the structure of work over several years. The former are mostly forgotten. The latter eventually change the coordinates we stand on.

I see harness engineering and Ralph loops as closer to the latter. The shift is from writing code directly, or giving one-off commands to an AI, toward building the environment in which agents work, allowing multiple agents to operate like a team, and eventually letting a higher-level AI design that environment itself. In AI research, the movement from hand-crafted features such as SIFT to deep learning, and then to meta learning, was also a movement up the abstraction stack. The current tool shift feels similar: work that humans used to define one piece at a time is being handed to higher layers of learning and execution.

That gradient needs to be understood. But not everyone needs to learn its implementation immediately. Understanding that harnesses matter is different from staying up all night with a configuration file that was released today. Knowing that agent environments will matter is different from copying this week's fashionable prompt chain. If I do not need to adopt it for my actual work right now, those specific settings will likely become easier and cheaper later. Early effort can become experience, but it is also a cost. As I wrote in [Getting Smug About Using AI](/en/posts/260514-ai-superpower-time-lag), one can live off other people's envy for a while; I did that ten years ago. But that does not change the essence of my work. There are people who should try AI tools first, and there are people and organizations that should adopt them a little later. That is a difference in timing, not rank.

The important thing is not to drift too far away. If I ignore the change completely, I miss the gradient. Saying "I can live fine without it" and refusing to look can make me miss even the right late timing. But if I stand too close to the turbulence of new tools, I get dragged around by the vibration. While flailing through every new feature, I may end up building unnecessary tools instead of advancing the work that actually matters.

There is no need to adopt everything first. Many features will disappear, some will change names, and the things that survive will come down in cheaper and kinder forms. FOMO is often not proof that I am behind. It is a natural delay caused by confusing the essence of my work with someone else's job. If the problem I need to solve is clear, I can see which tool is useful and when the most efficient moment to adopt it has arrived. So I should neither compete too early nor fall behind too late.
