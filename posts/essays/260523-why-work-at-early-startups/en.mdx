Why should a young person work at an early startup? Because it is more likely to make them rich? On average, probably not. The salary is lower, the company can die easily, and most stock options become worthless. Many people say early startups have no system, so it is better to join a large company with higher pay, a better working environment, and a structured system where a senior person can teach you. For what it is worth, I would not expect too much from “a structured system” or “a good senior mentor” in either a large company or a startup.

Then why work at an early startup? A good early startup gives four real rewards.
1. Responsibility comes early
2. You learn operating judgment from founders and early members
3. Your reputation is proven in the field
4. You build a high-trust network that leads to the next opportunity.

First, responsibility comes early. In a large company, a junior person often owns one small piece of work that has already been divided into parts. They are likely to repeat a narrow task inside a narrow field of view. In an early startup, even the pieces have not yet been defined. You meet customers, fix the product, persuade people, and decide what to give up this month. Responsibility coming early means being exposed to a broader part of the work and growing quickly. If you become the hero of that startup, there may even be economic upside from stock options.

Second, you learn the feel of work from founders and early members. Startup operations are not made from clean strategy documents. They are made under daily pressure. Which customer should we win first? Who should we hire, and who should we part ways with? When cash is short, what do we keep alive and what do we kill? What should we say to investors, customers, and teammates? Beside a good founder, you can watch messy inputs compress into one judgment and start to internalize that process. If the founder and early members are not good, there is no reason to join that early startup. Run.

Third, your reputation is proven in the field. Reputation is not made from a LinkedIn recommendation line or one or two people who will answer a reference check call. The strongest reputation comes from the voices of colleagues who worked closest to you through hard times. Whether you run from a crisis or stay, blame others when a customer is angry or solve the problem, hide when the product breaks or step forward, drain the team when it shakes or hold the center, all of that remains in others and in yourself like DNA. That kind of reputation is not social-media image making. It becomes a mirror through which you can look back at yourself.

Fourth, you build a high-trust network that leads to the next opportunity. It is a mistake to compare this with people who exchange business cards, say hello at conferences, or show off social-media acquaintances as a network. Many people in the startup world have high-trust networks without doing those things. They solved hard problems together, endured together, and created results together. That record makes them people founders preparing for the next challenge want to work with. Whether that person starts a company or takes a core mission somewhere else, economic reward will no longer be the biggest question.

There are many assets to build in life, but in a career the most important one may be this trust asset. People who work well until it is time to leave, then disappear while taking only their own advantage because “we will never see each other again,” may not understand this. They may actually never see those people again. They may interview well somewhere else and start a new life. But what matters more is the coordinate that gets carved inside. The question of how much responsibility I take in which situation accumulates inside me, and eventually decides whether I become someone who always runs away or someone who can save the people around me.

Many juniors chase the name of a large company, a well-ordered system, and the fantasy of a great mentor. But the truly valuable environment when you are young is different. It is a place where you can watch a strong founder or early member up close. You can see how that person meets customers, what they give up when money is short, what they say when the team shakes, and what judgment they make in front of investors and the market. The advantage of a small organization is distance. In a large organization, these scenes pass through documents and titles. In an early team, they appear directly in meeting rooms, Slack, and customer calls.

What accumulates there is not just one line on a resume. It is a high-trust relationship among people who passed through hard work together. People know who steps forward in a crisis, who leaves order behind until the end, and who repays trust with results rather than words. This knowledge has a different compression ratio from a LinkedIn recommendation or a business card exchanged at an event. The PayPal Mafia and many serial founders in Silicon Valley could do larger things partly because they had abundant team trust capital: people who had grown through pressure together and could try again with others who had already been verified.

In that sense, I hope young people leave each workplace in a way that turns the effort of several years into a real asset. Some leave without finishing the work because “we will not meet again anyway.” Some scrape out every last piece of company equipment, vacation, or benefit. Some package themselves with one career line even though the ending was not clean. That may work for a few early job changes, but it will not push them into positions of greater responsibility. Reputation is not a rumor told by strangers. It is testimony about responsibility from colleagues who truly lived through the work together.

I think of this whenever I want to run away. There are situations I could use as excuses. There are people I dislike. Maybe I could avoid that situation and that person and make an exciting fresh start somewhere else. But I do not want to leave that kind of record inside myself. My own time is precious, so I cannot promise to bind myself to responsibility forever. Still, I try today to create an ending that is at least not bad for everyone involved. I hope others do the same.
