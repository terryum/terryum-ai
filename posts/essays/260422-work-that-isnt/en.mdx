A superpower landed in my hands. Claude Code. As in [the story of clearing three years of tech debt in one day](/posts/260420-one-day-recovery), I can now spin up whatever I need in a few hours, without a developer. It feels like Frozen's Elsa — touch a spot and a building rises. My GitHub graveyard, dead for seven-plus years, started growing grass; repositories sprouted everywhere. Suddenly I was a software tycoon.

At any given moment, at least four sessions are running. The instant one stops, I feel like I've stopped working. So whenever one finishes, I send it off again. "This is slow, make it faster." "Check for security holes." "Clean up unused code and refactor it for reuse." Endless dusting of a room that's already spotless. I may rest, but the tokens cannot.

Early on, every custom tool I built nudged my productivity up a step. Now most of the high-yield work is done, and what remains is small change. It's like cleaning a room: at first the sweep clears the dust and you can finally work, but eventually you're scrubbing a spotless floor with no idea what to do next. Tokens keep burning, but I'm only burning them — not building anything with depth.

Then I caught a glimpse of myself ten years ago. As a PhD student at Waterloo, I'd come into the lab during Christmas break — when the campus was closed — just to keep the GPU running. Because I could rest, but the GPU couldn't. Sure, performance crept up by maybe 0.0001%. But was that the part of the research that actually mattered? It wasn't. I was running the GPU as a way of telling myself, "I'm doing AI research." Now I burn tokens to tell myself the same thing.

The pace at which I build new products has overtaken the pace at which I actually use them. I'm already busy enough using the few I have; what I really need is time away from the computer to read deeply and write. Instead I keep building, fixing things I don't use (and that aren't even broken yet), and studying harness engineering to build them better. I keep preaching that "in the AI era, Why and What matter more than How" — and meanwhile I've been buried in How.

Burning tokens isn't work. None of us brags about an electricity bill — "look how much I worked, look how brilliant I am." The people who actually push humanity forward have done it under an oil lamp, with a pen and a sheet of paper. Don't get addicted to building. A pile of work-that-isn't doesn't add up to work. Humans are supposed to do things that actually matter — not janitor for AI.
