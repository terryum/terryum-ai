Today, Jensen Huang, founder and CEO of NVIDIA, visited the Seoul National University Robot Institute. Two months ago, his daughter Madison Huang, NVIDIA's director of marketing, also [visited SNU RI](https://youtu.be/JJfRfHNw_5A?si=VYkIH8LniYe8BkN4). Seeing Jensen's visit follow so soon after made it clear how seriously NVIDIA sees Korea and Korean talent within its physical AI vision.

> Korea is very special. There are only a few countries in the world that have excellent electronics, mechanical engineering, manufacturing, and artificial intelligence capabilities at the same time. If these are not excellent, you cannot build the robots of the future. It is impossible.

The students chanting Jensen Huang's name felt almost like a Michael Jackson concert in Korea. But what stayed with me more was the speech itself. The way he delivered a future he had internalized, without a script, was overwhelming. I linked the [full transcript](/en/posts/260608-jensen-huang-snu-speech) and [video](https://youtu.be/fqeVRdsjuv8?si=cGvBnMZNBF3xm4RI) of Jensen Huang's SNU talk, and I recommend watching it.

Through today's talk, I felt that I learned what it means to be a founder who changes the world. Founders like Jensen Huang or Elon Musk do not simply operate inside the present. They present a world beyond it, sell a future that has not yet been built, and eventually pull the world in that direction. Jensen did not look like someone merely managing his company. He looked like someone managing the world toward the future he believes in. That may be why he keeps traveling around the world giving speeches.

NVIDIA's history shows how Jensen Huang has repeatedly bet on a strong vision of the future.

After founding NVIDIA in 1993, he committed to graphics cards in a CPU-centered market because he dreamed of better-looking games. That made NVIDIA one of the fastest-growing companies in history. Later, he redefined the graphics card not merely as a display device but as the center of a parallel computer: the GPU. CUDA was the key to that transition. The bet that a chip for drawing graphics could become a general-purpose computing device succeeded again through Bitcoin mining and the deep learning revolution.

After seeing the possibility of AI in deep learning, he believed that larger data centers, following scaling laws, would create AI closer to human intelligence. NVIDIA's first AI supercomputer, the DGX-1, found its first customer in OpenAI, then led by Elon Musk. That line eventually continued into the age of ChatGPT. Jensen Huang opened the age of graphics cards in the CPU era, then anticipated and built the age of deep learning and global data centers. He named NVIDIA's next market before it existed, then helped bring it into existence.

So what future is Jensen Huang betting on now? Today's talk made the answer clear: the line that runs from agentic computers to physical AI.

He is trying to change the unit of the computer again. In the past, code plus an operating system made computers and applications. Now, LLM plus harness creates AI, opening the era of a new computer: the agentic computer. Where operating systems once managed files, memory, I/O, and scheduling, harnesses will manage an agent's memory, permissions, tool calls, and sandbox. The place where an OS used to execute programs will be replaced by a harness calling an LLM.

He strongly predicted that the screens, keyboards, mice, and file systems we have used for more than 40 years will change because of AI. He also said that this fall he will introduce a new form of PC together with Microsoft's Satya Nadella. That PC will be less a device where a person launches apps and more a local execution environment where agents operate computers on a person's behalf. Today's event was called Build-a-Claw, and Jensen's almost missionary promotion of OpenClaw seems aimed at creating an NVIDIA future in which every PC contains LLM agents and the GPU environment they need.

The next bet is the embodied agent: physical AI. Jensen said that when a digital agent receives a physical body, it becomes a robot. Just as an LLM with a harness uses software tools, a robot with a body will use tools in the real world. Robots will move hands, read cameras, sense force, talk to equipment in factories, retry when they fail, and leave successful actions behind as skills for future work. They become physical agents that evolve through contact with reality.

Jensen said that if the past 40 years were a great era for software engineers, the next era may be the best time to study mechanical engineering. As someone who studied mechanical engineering as an undergraduate and AI and robotics in graduate school, I was genuinely happy to hear that shout-out. Everyone there, including the professors and mechanical engineering students, cheered. I agree with him. The value of software that can be copied with one click will keep moving toward zero. What humans should focus on is reality, and I believe that reality lives in robot bodies and manufacturing sites.

This larger technical landscape is also connected to the [NVIDIA Physical AI for Manufacturing Robotics survey](https://www.terryum.ai/en/surveys/survey-nvidia-physical-ai-robotics) I am separately organizing. Physical AI, as NVIDIA describes it, is not the intelligence of a single robot. It is a full computing stack trained in data centers, validated in simulators, and deployed to edge devices and manufacturing floors.

In that sense, I hope the Seoul National University Robot Institute, where I studied, leads Korea's manufacturing physical AI from the center of academia. I also hope Cosmax, the heart of K-beauty manufacturing where I work, becomes an arena where advanced robots compete and improve. Formulation, filling, packaging, inspection, logistics, and even the delicate motions of skilled human hands can all become data. When the skilled work and work environments inside Cosmax meet a talent pool like SNU RI, I believe they can create a moment of physical AI innovation. Today, I once again resolve not to watch that wave from the shore, but to become one of its main actors.
