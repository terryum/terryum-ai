## TL;DR

DexForce computes force-informed action targets from contact forces measured during kinesthetic demonstrations. A single spring model parameter achieves 76% average success across six dexterous manipulation tasks, while policies ignoring forces achieve near-zero success.

## Problem

Dexterous manipulation requires applying proper forces at correct contact locations and timings. Teleoperation-based demonstration collection suffers from two fundamental limitations: (1) kinematic differences between human and robot hands make intuitive control difficult, and (2) lack of haptic feedback makes force regulation impossible. As a result, observed robot fingertip positions alone lose critical force information — the same position is recorded regardless of how much force is applied during contact. DexForce addresses this by combining kinesthetic teaching with 6-axis force-torque sensors.

## Key Idea

DexForce's core insight is remarkably simple: **offset target positions proportionally to contact forces**.

- **Spring model**: $x_f = x_o + k_f \cdot f$ — adds force ($f$) × stiffness coefficient ($k_f$) to observed position ($x_o$) to compute force-informed target ($x_f$)
- **Single parameter**: $k_f = 0.0045$ applied identically across all tasks (no task-specific tuning required)
- **Two-stage demonstration**: (1) Kinesthetic teaching records position + force → computes force-informed targets, (2) Robot-only replay collects training observations (operator hand removed)
- **Impedance control execution**: Learned policy outputs force-informed targets, and the impedance controller naturally generates appropriate contact forces

### Cartesian Impedance Control

DexForce builds on Cartesian impedance control. Control force is computed as $F = k_p(x_d - x_c) - k_v \dot{x}_c$ ($k_p=310$, $k_v=2$), converted to joint torques via $\tau = J^\top F + g$.

### Force-Informed Target Computation

During kinesthetic demonstrations, fingertip positions and contact forces are recorded simultaneously. The key observation is that under impedance control, observed positions during contact differ from actual targets — because the contacted object pushes the fingertip back. The spring model $x_f = x_o + k_f \cdot f$ compensates for this difference.

### Two-Stage Demonstration Collection

**Stage 1**: Records T-timestep position and force sequences from kinesthetic demonstration, computes force-informed target sequence via the spring model.

**Stage 2**: Replays the computed target sequence through the impedance controller while recording robot-only observations (RGB, force, moments). This stage produces data under identical conditions to test time (no operator hand in scene).

### Policy Architecture

Uses CNN-based Diffusion Policy with ResNet18 image encoder. Observations consist of RGB images + 6-axis force-torque data, recorded at 30Hz → downsampled to 10Hz, with observation horizon 2, action prediction horizon 16, and execution horizon 8 timesteps.

## Main Results

- **Force-informed actions are critical**: 76% average success rate across six tasks. Policies trained on observed positions without force achieve **near-zero** success — contact occurs but necessary forces are not generated
- **Force observations help**: RGB + 6-axis force outperforms RGB-only on all tasks. Statistically significant improvements on AirPods opening (10% → 70%, +60%p) and nut unscrewing (33% → 78%, +45%p). Binary contact signals (0/1) underperform 6-axis force data on all tasks
- **$k_f$ sensitivity**: Single $k_f=0.0045$ falls within the permissible range for all six tasks. Precision tasks (AirPods, nut) have narrower permissible ranges, consistent with which tasks benefit most from force observations

## Achievements and Limitations

**Achievements**
- A single spring model with one parameter achieves consistent performance across six diverse dexterous manipulation tasks
- Systematically demonstrates that force information matters in both actions and observations through controlled experiments
- Effective policy learning from as few as 5-10 demonstrations

**Limitations**
- Kinesthetic teaching is limited to one demonstration per operator session, making large-scale data collection inefficient
- Only two fingers can be simultaneously controlled (physical constraint of hand-guided demonstration)
- Performance degrades in OOD settings (~50% success), showing generalization limitations

🤖 The spring model's simplicity is both its strength and limitation. It works empirically without explicitly modeling soft contact or friction, but may struggle with more complex contact dynamics (e.g., deformable object manipulation). Additionally, the CoinFT 6-axis force-torque sensors are required hardware, limiting applicability to existing robot systems without such sensors.
