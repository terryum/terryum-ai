## TL;DR

The bottleneck in humanoid sim-to-real RL is no longer only slow simulation. Once strong domain randomization and high-dimensional whole-body control enter the loop, training can still become unstable and time-consuming. This paper stabilizes FastSAC and FastTD3 for humanoid control, training Unitree G1 and Booster T1 locomotion policies in 15 minutes on a single RTX 4090 and deploying them to real robots.

<Video src="https://younggyo.me/fastsac-humanoid/static/videos/teaser.mov" caption="Project teaser: Unitree G1 and Booster T1 locomotion and whole-body tracking demos trained with FastSAC/FastTD3." type="direct" />

## Problem

Massively parallel simulation has already reduced wall-clock training time for robot RL. Humanoids remain difficult because they are high-dimensional, unstable, and sensitive to modeling errors. Add rough terrain, push perturbations, action delays, randomized dynamics, and curriculum tuning, and exploration becomes hard again. In a real sim-to-real workflow, every hardware failure forces another simulation iteration, so hours-long training runs slow down the entire development loop.

The paper asks a concrete question: **can humanoid locomotion policy learning become a 15-minute sim-to-real iteration loop?** The authors answer by moving away from the PPO-centered on-policy default and exploiting the data reuse of off-policy RL at massive simulation scale.

## Core Idea

The main contribution is less “FastSAC or FastTD3 alone” than the recipe that makes them usable for humanoid sim-to-real.

- **Off-policy RL at scale**: use FastSAC/FastTD3 instead of PPO to reuse simulation rollout data more aggressively.
- **Strong domain randomization**: randomize dynamics, rough terrain, push perturbations, action delays, PD gains, mass, friction, and center of mass.
- **Minimalist reward design**: use essential terms such as velocity tracking, foot height, action smoothness, and alive reward instead of a heavily shaped 20-plus-term reward.
- **Stability details**: tune joint-limit-aware action bounds, observation/layer normalization, critic updates, entropy, and exploration hyperparameters for humanoid tasks.

<Collapsible title="Method Details">

FastSAC and FastTD3 are off-policy variants of SAC and TD3 optimized for large-scale parallel simulation. This paper adapts them to full-body humanoid locomotion and whole-body tracking.

First, the action bounds are tied to joint limits. With tanh policies in SAC/TD3, bounds that are too narrow limit torque, while bounds that are too broad destabilize exploration. The paper uses joint-limit-aware action bounds as the default, while noting that unbounded action spaces can help in some failure cases.

Second, normalization matters. Observation normalization is used, and layer normalization is especially helpful for high-dimensional tasks. On the critic side, the authors find that averaging Q-values can outperform clipped double Q-learning in this setup.

Third, hyperparameters differ by task. A lower discount factor, $\gamma=0.97$, helps simple velocity tracking, while $\gamma=0.99$ is better for harder whole-body tracking. For FastSAC, automatic entropy tuning consistently beats fixed alpha values, with different target entropy choices for locomotion and WBT.

Finally, rewards are intentionally simple. Locomotion uses linear/angular velocity tracking, foot-height tracking, orientation/torque/action-rate penalties, and an alive reward. Penalty weights are ramped by curriculum as episode length increases. The point is not just elegance: simpler rewards make hyperparameter sweeps and sim-to-real iteration faster.

</Collapsible>

## Results

The headline result is literal: the authors train checkpoints for **15 minutes on a single RTX 4090 GPU** and deploy them to Unitree G1 and Booster T1 hardware. Training includes rough terrain, randomized dynamics, and push perturbations.

<Figure src="./fig-3.png" caption="Figure 3: G1/T1 locomotion results. FastSAC and FastTD3 improve velocity tracking faster than PPO under rough terrain and Push-Strong settings." number={3} />

Key results:

- **15-minute training**: G1/T1 locomotion policies are trained in 15 minutes on a single RTX 4090.
- **Strong domain randomization**: Push-Strong applies perturbations every 1-3 seconds, while non-Push-Strong tasks apply pushes every 5-10 seconds.
- **Whole-body tracking**: with 4xL40s GPUs and 16,384 parallel environments, FastSAC/FastTD3 reach Dance, Box Lifting, and Push tracking performance faster than PPO.
- **Real hardware deployment**: the project page shows Unitree G1 and Booster T1 locomotion, plus Unitree G1 whole-body tracking.

## Achievements and Limitations

What the paper newly achieves:

- It shows that off-policy RL can be a practical recipe for humanoid sim-to-real, not just a fast but unstable alternative.
- It turns humanoid policy iteration into a 15-minute loop on a single RTX 4090 for locomotion.
- It stabilizes FastSAC through normalization, critic choices, entropy tuning, reward design, and curriculum.
- It demonstrates the recipe on real Unitree G1 and Booster T1 robots and releases the Holosoma implementation.

Limitations:

- Author note: the performance differences between FastSAC and FastTD3 in whole-body tracking are not fully explained yet and require broader task studies.
- Author note: future off-policy RL improvements may further improve performance and stability.
- 🤖 Reproducibility: the 15-minute number is a system-level result, not just an algorithmic speed claim. Simulator throughput, reward/curriculum choices, domain randomization, normalization, action bounds, and hardware setup all have to line up for another lab to reproduce it.
