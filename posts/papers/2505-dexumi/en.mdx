## TL;DR

Teaching dexterous manipulation to robot hands faces two fundamental barriers: **kinematic gap** and **visual gap**. DexUMI resolves both simultaneously — the former through a wearable exoskeleton, the latter through SAM2 + AI inpainting — achieving an average 86% task success rate across two different robot hand platforms.

## Problem

Dexterous robot manipulation research is bottlenecked by the difficulty of collecting large-scale, high-quality demonstration data. While imitation learning works well for low-DoF grippers, extending it to 5-finger robot hands introduces two simultaneous problems. **Kinematic gap**: human and robot hands differ in joint structure, degrees of freedom, and finger proportions, making direct control mapping infeasible. **Visual gap**: data collected by humans directly manipulating objects shows human hands — not robot hands — causing distribution shift when a policy trained on this data runs on an actual robot. Existing teleoperation approaches partially address the kinematic gap, but remain data-collection-inefficient and leave the visual gap unresolved.

## Core Idea

DexUMI resolves both gaps via separate **hardware adaptation** and **software adaptation** layers:

- **Hardware adaptation (kinematic gap)**: A wearable exoskeleton designed via bi-level optimization to match the target robot hand's fingertip workspace, aligning the joint-to-fingertip mapping while remaining ergonomically wearable
- **Software adaptation (visual gap)**: SAM2 segments the exoskeleton out of collected demonstration videos; the background is inpainted; then the corresponding joint actions are replayed on the actual robot hand and photographed to replace the human hand appearance
- **Relative action representation**: Using relative hand actions (rather than absolute joint positions) as policy output yields more precise, better-coordinated multi-finger movements
- **Tactile feedback integration**: FSR-based tactile sensors improve performance on contact-rich tasks that are difficult to distinguish visually

### Hardware Optimization

The exoskeleton design is determined via bi-level optimization. The goal is to find design parameters $p$ (joint positions, linkage lengths) that maximize the workspace similarity between the exoskeleton fingertip workspace $W_{\text{exo}}^{\text{tip}}(p)$ and the robot hand workspace $W_{\text{robot}}^{\text{tip}}$:

$$\max_{p} \, S\!\left(W_{\text{exo}}^{\text{tip}}(p),\, W_{\text{robot}}^{\text{tip}}\right)$$

To avoid thumb collision, a mechanism optimization step moves the exoskeleton thumb backward while preserving the original fingertip and joint mapping in SE(3) space. Each finger joint uses a rotary encoder to estimate joint angle from ADC voltage:

$$\alpha_{\text{joint}} = \frac{V_{\text{ADC}}}{V_{\text{supply}}} \times 360°$$

Tactile force is measured by converting FSR sensor resistance change via a voltage divider: $F = k\!\left(\frac{V_{\text{supply}}}{V_{\text{ADC}}} - 1\right)$

### Software Pipeline

1. **Segmentation**: SAM2 extracts exoskeleton regions from collected video frames
2. **Background Inpainting**: Fills removed regions with plausible background
3. **Robot Hand Rendering**: Replays corresponding joint commands on the real robot hand and captures images from the same camera position
4. **Occlusion Handling**: Preserves natural occlusion relationships during hand-object interactions

## Key Results

Per-task success rates from Table 1 (Inspire robot hand):

- **Cube Picking**: 100% — validates basic picking precision
- **Egg Carton**: 85% — requires multi-finger coordination
- **Tea Picking with Tool**: 100% — millimeter-level contact-rich manipulation
- **Kitchen (Knob / Pan / Salt)**: 100% / 85% / 95% — long-horizon, high-precision task chain

On the XHand (fully-actuated) platform, 85–90% success rates demonstrate **platform-agnostic generality**. Data collection throughput is **3.2× higher** than teleoperation (measured over 15-minute windows).

## Achievements and Limitations

**What's newly achieved:**
- First system to resolve kinematic gap and visual gap **simultaneously in a single integrated pipeline**
- High success rates demonstrated across two architecturally distinct robot hands (underactuated Inspire and fully-actuated XHand)
- 3.2× data collection efficiency gain, partially alleviating the data bottleneck in dexterous manipulation learning

**Limitations:**
- Per-robot-hand exoskeleton design and fabrication required (limits scalability)
- Software adaptation (inpainting) still requires real robot hardware in the loop; lighting effects cannot be fully reproduced
- Camera must be rigidly attached to the robot hand/exoskeleton — a structural constraint on deployment flexibility
- 🤖 All experiments use a single fixed-viewpoint camera setup; generalization to varied camera placements or unstructured environments remains unverified
