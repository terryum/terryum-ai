## TL;DR

How can sample-efficient visuotactile policies be learned given the high cost of tactile data collection? EquiTac exploits the SO(2) symmetry inherent in in-hand object rotation, using an equivariant network to predict corrective rotations from tactile surface normal maps. It achieves 90% success on flower arrangement with only 10 demonstrations and zero-shot generalization to unseen orientations.

## Problem

Visuotactile policy learning supplements vision-only policies with information that vision alone cannot provide — contact forces, slip, surface geometry. However, real-world tactile data collection is **extremely expensive** — sensor mounting, calibration, and demonstration are all manual. Therefore, **sample efficiency** is the key requirement. Existing approaches require large demonstration datasets to generalize across orientation variations.

## Key Idea

EquiTac's core insight is that **when an object rotates in-hand, the tactile normal map co-rotates**. This SO(2) equivariance is exploited through three components:

- **Surface Normal Map Reconstruction**: Converts raw tactile RGB images to normal maps $N \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times H \times W}$. In-plane components $(n_x, n_y)$ rotate as 2-vectors while $n_z$ remains invariant
- **SO(2)-Equivariant Network**: $C_8$-equivariant CNN predicts target yaw from normal maps. Satisfies $\phi(g \cdot N) = g \cdot \phi(N)$ for zero-shot generalization to unlearned orientations
- **Residual Rotation Correction**: Lightweight equivariant module provides high-frequency yaw corrections on top of a Flow Matching base policy, with proportional gain $K_p$ for gradual adjustment

<Collapsible title="Detailed Methods">

### Surface Normal Map Reconstruction

MLP predicts surface gradients from color and spatial location $(R,G,B,U,V)$ using GelSight calibration with a metal sphere. Key property: when object rotates in-plane by angle $\alpha$, normal map $(n_x, n_y)$ components rotate identically.

### Double-Angle Representation

Predicts $(cos\, 2\alpha_t, sin\, 2\alpha_t)$ to prevent $\pi$-rotation ambiguity (identical object appearance at 180°). Restricts corrections to the forward semicircle.

### Hierarchical Policy Composition

Base policy $\pi_b$ generates full trajectories from visual observations at low frequency. EquiTac module provides real-time yaw corrections from tactile at high frequency. Complementary operation without modifying the base policy.

</Collapsible>

## Key Results

- **Angular estimation error**: EquiTac mean **2.9°** (w/o equivariance: 13.5°, w/o normal map: 27.4°)
- **Real-world tasks (with orientation variation)**:
  - Drawing on paper: EquiTac **90-95%** (FM+RGB: 0-5%)
  - Flower arrangement: EquiTac **85-90%** (FM+RGB: 0-5%)
  - Straw classification: EquiTac **85-90%** (FM+RGB: 25-40%)
  - Pouring tea: EquiTac **95%** (FM+RGB: 40-45%)
- **Sample efficiency**: **90%** success on flower arrangement with only 10 demonstrations — baseline reaches max 75% with 60 demos
- **Generalization**: Maintains 4.1-5.6° error on unseen screw geometries

## Achievements and Limitations

**Key achievements**
- **First systematic exploitation of SO(2) equivariance** in tactile normal maps — zero-shot generalization to unseen orientations
- **90% success from just 10 demonstrations** — structurally addresses tactile data collection cost
- **Hierarchical residual correction** enables non-invasive addition of tactile capability to existing policies

**Limitations**
- Only exploits SO(2) in-plane rotation — extension to full SE(3) motions remains unaddressed
- Real-world multimodal data collection cost still high
- Tactile simulation not explored
- 🤖 Limited to 2-DOF gripper yaw correction; scalability to dexterous hand manipulation (e.g., finger repositioning) unverified
