## TL;DR

The inability of robotic hands to grasp thin, deformable objects like paper is resolved by PP-Tac — a pipeline combining a round tactile sensor (R-Tac), a slip-detection CNN, online force control, and a diffusion-based policy — achieving 87.5% success across diverse object materials and terrain configurations.

## The Problem

Robots have largely solved rigid object grasping, but thin, flat, deformable objects — paper, cloth, plastic bags — remain stubbornly difficult. Two gaps make this hard: (1) the lack of suitable perception for reliable state estimation under diverse appearances, and (2) the absence of grasp motion planning that can handle buckling and deformation dynamics. Prior tactile sensor work mostly addressed slip detection or force estimation in isolation, without integrating these signals into a closed-loop system capable of adapting to varied terrain, material, and stiffness. PP-Tac is the first attempt to directly tackle the problem of picking paper-like deformable objects using a tactile dexterous hand.

## Core Idea

PP-Tac integrates four components into a unified pipeline:

- **R-Tac tactile sensor**: A round-shaped, camera-based gel sensor providing omnidirectional tactile sensing. Gel surface deformation is captured as an image and converted to a depth/normal map in under 10 ms.
- **Slip detection network**: A CNN binary classifier that computes slip probability $P_\text{slip}$ from a reference no-contact image and five recent tactile frames. Threshold 0.75 found empirically optimal.
- **Online frictional force control**: A compliant closed-loop controller that incrementally increases contact force when slip is detected.
- **Diffusion-based policy**: A Transformer encoder that infers the next action conditioned on robot proprioception and the target contact force.

## Methods in Detail

<Collapsible title="Methods in Detail">

**Hardware**

Four R-Tac sensors are mounted as the distal links of a four-fingered dexterous hand. Each R-Tac consists of a hemispherical gel dome, a small camera, and LED illumination. The round shape ensures uniform tactile sensitivity in all directions.

**Depth/Normal Map Reconstruction**

Tactile images are mapped to depth maps using camera intrinsics/extrinsics calibrated via an indentation setup. Photometric Stereo reconstructs surface normals. Full pipeline latency: < 10 ms.

**Slip Detection**

$$P_\text{slip} = f_\theta(I_\text{ref}, I_{t-4}, I_{t-3}, I_{t-2}, I_{t-1}, I_t)$$

Training set: 40% slip, 60% non-slip samples, manually annotated. Loss: binary cross-entropy. Threshold 0.75 set empirically.

**Trajectory Synthesis**

Pinching trajectories are synthesized from a physics-based buckling model: fingertips slide along the terrain surface, and the spacing between waypoints controls sensor deformation depth. These synthesized trajectories form the training dataset for the diffusion policy.

**Diffusion Policy**

4-layer Transformer encoder, latent dimension 512, 4 attention heads. Sequences of length 10 are used for the diffusion process. Trained for ~600,000 iterations on a single RTX 4090.

## Results

Experiments cover 4 objects (paper, plastic bag, cloth, kraft paper bag) × 4 terrains (plane, slope, book placement, random complex terrain) = 16 conditions:

- **PP-Tac overall success rate: 87.5%**
- **Gripper\* (bi-finger teleoperation)**: structurally unsuitable for thin flat objects
- **Open-loop baseline** (PP-Tac trajectory + compliant control, no slip/force feedback): significantly lower success
- **Model-based force tracking** (PP-Tac trajectory + tactile-based force tracking): intermediate performance

## Contributions and Limitations

**What's new**
- **First** dexterous tactile hand system demonstrated on paper-like thin deformable objects (authors' claim)
- Round sensor design + terrain-adaptive trajectory synthesis enables operation on slopes, books, and random terrain
- Closed-loop integration of slip detection → force control → diffusion policy for real-time adaptation

**Limitations**
- Constrained to the specific dexterous hand morphology and fingertip-mounted sensor form factor — not immediately portable to other platforms
- Trajectory synthesis relies on a buckling dynamics model assuming flat thin objects — generalization to 3D deformables or highly compliant cloth is uncertain
- 🤖 The 87.5% aggregate success rate averages across all 16 conditions; the per-object, per-terrain distribution is not fully reported — making it hard to assess which scenarios remain unsolved and whether the system is robust enough for unstructured real-world deployment
