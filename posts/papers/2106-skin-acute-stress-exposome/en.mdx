## TL;DR

This review turns acute exposome stress from a list of irritants into a map of **how skin homeostasis mobilizes several functions at once**. The key point is that acute responses can be protective, but when repeated or layered on chronic exposure, they can produce barrier impairment, inflammation, pigmentation changes, disease flares, infection risk, scalp sensitivity, and hair shedding.

## Questions and Scope

The authors define the skin exposome as the total non-genetic set of environmental and internal exposures that disturb skin homeostasis. Earlier reviews had already covered the chronic effects of UV, pollution, smoking, nutrition, and stress, especially in relation to skin aging. This paper focuses instead on short-term acute exposure, typically minutes to hours and generally no longer than a week. The review asks which stressors perturb which skin functions, which biological mechanisms respond immediately, and which clinical signs become visible.

## Landscape Taxonomy

The paper organizes acute stress responses around six skin functions.

- **Physical barrier**: the stratum corneum, lipid lamellae, tight junctions, TEWL, hydration, and desquamation connect to dryness, tightness, and sensitivity.
- **Pigmentation**: UVB-DNA damage-p53-POMC-MC1R signaling and visible-light-Opsin 3 signaling connect to tanning, PIHP, and melasma worsening.
- **Defences**: antioxidant response, osmolyte strategy, DNA repair, innate/adaptive immunity, and microbiome maintenance connect to ROS, cytokines, DAMP/TLR signaling, and infection risk.
- **Structure**: extracellular matrix, hair follicles, sebaceous glands, and sweat glands connect to sebum, sweat, telogen effluvium, wrinkles, and laxity.
- **Neuroendocrine signaling**: sympathetic, HPA, cholinergic, neuropeptide, and mast-cell axes connect to pruritus, pain, erythema, and oedema.
- **Thermoregulation**: blood-vessel and fat-mediated vasoconstriction or flushing connect to pale skin, redness, rosacea flare, and temperature change.

### External stressors: UV, pollution, and weather

Solar radiation can trigger DNA damage, ROS, inflammation, photoimmunosuppression, microbiome change, and pigmentation. Pollution works through ozone, particulate matter, VOCs, and diesel exhaust particles, producing oxidative stress, lipid peroxidation, antioxidant depletion, microbiome alteration, acne flares, and atopic dermatitis flares. UV and pollution are not independent in practice: they can add tissue peroxidation and oxinflammation in the stratum corneum. Abrupt humidity and temperature shifts affect TEWL, hydration, mast cells, pro-inflammatory cytokines, and cortisol response, increasing dryness and reactivity to irritants or allergens.

### Internal stressors: psychosocial stress, sleep, nutrition, and hormones

Psychosocial stress can activate sympathetic, HPA, neuropeptidergic, and cholinergic axes. That response may be immune-enhancing and homeostatic, but when intense or poorly buffered it can manifest as erythema, oedema, pruritus, pain, acne, rosacea, psoriasis, and atopic dermatitis flares. Sleep deprivation interacts with cortisol, inflammatory cytokines, oxidative stress, and circadian imbalance, contributing to barrier impairment, dullness, itch, and disease flare. Nutrition and alcohol act through glycaemic load, dairy and fat patterns, alcohol/spicy-food activation of TRP channels, and gut microbiome shifts. Hormonal variation affects sebum, sweat, telogen effluvium, acne, atopic dermatitis, and neuropeptide modulation.

### Medical and lifestyle stressors: procedures and pandemic-related exposure

Peels, botulinum toxin, fillers, lasers, microdermabrasion, and topical retinoids can cause barrier disruption, inflammation, irritation, PIHP, and superinfection risk. In the COVID-19 context, masks, gloves, and repeated hand sanitization increased TEWL, temperature, erythema, sebum, humidity, and friction, connecting practical protective behavior to irritant dermatitis, acne, rosacea flare, dryness, and sensitivity.

## Datasets and Evaluation Practices

This is closer to a narrative review than a systematic review or meta-analysis. Its evidence base mixes human clinical observations, short exposure trials, ex vivo skin models, mouse studies, retrospective time-series analyses, and mechanistic dermatology literature. The main output is not a benchmark but Table 1, which links each acute stressor to **affected skin function - biological mechanism - clinical manifestation**.

## Open Problems and Future Directions

- **Separating multiple stressors**: real skin is exposed to UV, pollution, humidity, sleep loss, and psychosocial stress together, so better designs are needed to isolate individual effects.
- **Acute-to-chronic transition**: the field needs to define when a protective acute response becomes repeated exposure, inflammaging, skin aging, or chronic dermatosis flare.
- **Individual response models**: genome, phototype, chronic exposome background, disease history, and microbiome state can all change the response to the same exposure.
- **Shared biomarkers**: endpoints such as TEWL, erythema, sebum, cytokines, ROS, microbiome shift, neuropeptides, and cortisol need more standardized use across stressor types.
- **Prevention trials**: sun protection, sleep, diet, stress management, moisturizers, antioxidants, anti-pollution products, and probiotics need clearer tests against acute-response endpoints.
- 🤖 **AI analysis limit**: the review gives a useful map, but the evidence quality and study design vary strongly by stressor, so comparing effect size across axes would overstate the data.

## Achievements and Limitations

**What it adds**
- It provides a practical map connecting acute exposome stress to six skin functions and visible clinical manifestations.
- It unifies UV, pollution, psychosocial stress, sleep, diet, hormones, procedures, and mask use as perturbations of skin homeostasis.
- It makes short-term minutes-to-days responses a distinct topic, separate from chronic skin aging exposome research.

**Limitations**
- The evidence is heterogeneous and not quantitatively synthesized through a systematic review or meta-analysis.
- Some clinical manifestations are delayed flares or subclinical mechanisms rather than immediate visible signs.
- The advisory board and editorial support were funded by Vichy/L'Oreal-related entities, so cosmetic prevention implications should be read with that conflict-of-interest context in mind.
