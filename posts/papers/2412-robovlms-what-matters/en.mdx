## TL;DR

A VLA design guidebook from 8 VLM backbones × 4 policy architectures × 600+ experiments. The verdict: KosMos/Paligemma backbone, continuous actions, a separate policy head, and OXE as post-train. All combinations are bundled into one open-source meta-builder, RoboVLMs.

## Problem

Since RT-2 in 2023, VLA (Vision-Language-Action) models have exploded — but each new work picks (1) a VLM backbone, (2) an action formulation (discrete tokens vs continuous, with or without history integration), and (3) a way of mixing in cross-embodiment data like Open X-Embodiment **on its own terms**. That makes it nearly impossible to answer "which VLA is actually better?" honestly, and forces follow-up researchers to build new VLAs without knowing what to keep and what to change.

This paper decomposes the VLA design space along three axes — (a) backbone choice, (b) policy structure (action space × history aggregation), and (c) cross-embodiment data integration timing — and quantifies each axis through 600+ controlled experiments using the same data, same evaluation protocol, and the same framework. The output is a one-line, current-best-practice recipe.

## Key Idea

**RoboVLMs: a meta VLA builder that bundles every design combination into one framework.**

Core components:

- **A VLA taxonomy (Figure 2)**: every existing VLA is placed along two axes — the vertical axis is action space (discrete tokens vs continuous), the horizontal axis is whether history is integrated (one-step vs history). For history-integrating VLAs, two sub-flavors split off: `interleaved` (obs and actions are interleaved inside the VLM context) vs `policy head` (the VLM processes each timestep's observation independently and a separate policy head aggregates history before predicting actions).
- **Three core questions**:
  1. **Which VLM backbone?** — eight VLMs (LLaVA, Flamingo, KosMos, Paligemma, etc.) are slotted into the same policy structure and compared. Result: **KosMos and Paligemma** consistently sit at the top.
  2. **How to formulate the VLA?** — 12 configurations (3 VLMs × 4 structures) are trained on the same data. Result: **continuous > discrete actions**, **history > one-step**, and within history aggregation, **Policy Head (P.H.) > Interleaved**.
  3. **When to use cross-embodiment (OXE) data?** — three timings (pre-train, fine-tune, post-train) are compared. Result: in-domain training first, then OXE **post-training** wins (the same recipe π₀ adopts).
- **The winning combination — KosMos P.H.**: KosMos backbone + Policy Head structure + continuous actions + OXE post-train tops Octo-Base, OpenVLA, and the broader VLA family across CALVIN, SimplerEnv, and 20 real-robot tasks.

Headline observation: decoder-only VLMs trained on rich vision-language data (KosMos, Paligemma) lead VLA performance — not raw model size, but **VL pre-training breadth and grounding ability** are the deciding factors.

**2. VLA formulation — four-way structure comparison**

The 2×2 of action space × history handling produces:

- **Disc + One-step**: RT-2 style (discrete-token autoregression, current observation only).
- **Disc + History**: RT-2 plus an observation history (interleaved).
- **Cont + Interleaved**: continuous actions, with observation and action tokens interleaved inside the VLM context window.
- **Cont + Policy Head (P.H.)**: continuous actions, with the VLM processing each observation separately and a dedicated policy head aggregating history to predict an action chunk.

All three VLMs (LLaVA, Flamingo, KosMos) are paired with all four structures → 12 models trained and compared on CALVIN ABCD. Result: **Cont + P.H. wins across every backbone.** The interleaved structure distorts the VLM's token distribution and breaks pre-trained vision-language alignment.

**3. Policy Head design details**

- The VLM converts each timestep's (obs, language) into a token sequence.
- The policy head consumes N timesteps of VLM output tokens and predicts an action chunk.
- Candidate heads — GPT-style decoder, LSTM, transformer — are compared in Appendix G. Differences exist, **but they are smaller than the VLM backbone effect**.
- Training uses regression loss over action chunks (a diffusion head would be a drop-in alternative). This study uses regression.

**4. Cross-embodiment data — three timing strategies**

- **Pre-train**: train on OXE first, then in-domain fine-tune (the RT-2 / OpenVLA / Octo pattern).
- **Bridge fine-tune (in-domain only)**: a baseline without OXE.
- **Post-train**: in-domain fine-tune first, then continue training on OXE (the π₀ pattern).

**Post-train wins** in both WidowX+Bridge and Google Robot environments. Pre-train alone doesn't adapt enough to the in-domain distribution; post-train absorbs OXE's diversity while preserving in-domain priorities.

**5. Training pipeline**

- Data: CALVIN (simulated kitchen manipulation), Bridge V2 (WidowX), SimplerEnv (Google Robot), and a real-robot benchmark of 20 tasks — 74K trajectories in total.
- All RoboVLMs variants share the same data and the same training schedule — the only varying axes are (backbone, structure, OXE integration timing).
- Evaluation metrics: CALVIN reports 5-task chain success and average task length; SimplerEnv reports task success rates; real-robot evaluation runs 5 rollouts × 20 tasks across four generalization setups.

## Key Results

<Figure src="./fig-5.png" alt="Real-world evaluation setup" caption="The illustration of the experimental settings in real-world experiments. - **CALVIN ABCD → D**: KosMos P.H. (RoboVLMs) becomes SOTA within just 5 training epochs, with an average task length of 4.25 — clearing RoboFlamingo, OpenVLA, and other baselines.
- **SimplerEnv (WidowX+Bridge, Google Robot)**: KosMos P.H. is the best VLA structure observed under matched training steps, surpassing the prior VLA family (RT-2-X, Octo, OpenVLA).
- **20 real-robot tasks**: against fine-tuned Octo-Base and OpenVLA, KosMos P.H. wins every evaluation setup (Simple / Unseen Distractor / Unseen Target Object / Unseen Background / Novel Skill), with the largest gap on Simple and Unseen Background.
- **Emergent self-correction**: even though the training data contains no such examples, KosMos P.H. (RoboVLMs) produces self-correcting rollouts — e.g. on "Open The Oven," when the first attempt misses the handle, the policy re-positions the end-effector and grabs the handle on the second attempt.

### Key design decisions, in one breath

<Figure src="./fig-8.png" alt="VL pre-training ablation" caption="Ablation study of VLAs for vision-language pre-train on different settings of the CALVIN benchmark. 'P.H.' denotes policy head. 'No VL' suggests models without VL pre-training. '5x' represents training with 5x re-generated training data. Full results on different training setting and data scale are shown in Appendix E." number={8} />

- **Backbone**: VL pre-training scale and grounding capability dominate — KosMos / Paligemma > LLaVA / Flamingo > smaller decoder VLMs. The "No VL" baseline (trained from scratch without VL pre-training) loses by a wide margin even when given 5× the training data.
- **Action space**: continuous > discrete. Autoregressive discrete tokens hit a ceiling on fine-grained precise manipulation.
- **History aggregation**: history > one-step, and within history, Policy Head > Interleaved. Interleaving damages the VLM's pre-trained token distribution.
- **OXE data integration**: post-train > fine-tune > pre-train. Learning in-domain first, then absorbing diversity, is the effective order.

<Figure src="./fig-10.png" alt="Self-correction rollout visualization" caption="Visualization for rollouts that the best setting VLA built by RoboVLMs emerges the ability of self-correction. For instance, in the Open The Oven task, the robot's first attempt does not reach the oven handle, and it adjusts the end-effector position to re-locate the handle at the second attempt. Note that the training dataset does not contain this kind of data." number={10} />

## Achievements and Limitations

### What this work achieved

- **A quantitative decomposition of the VLA design space**: 8 VLMs × 4 policy structures × 3 OXE integration strategies, 600+ runs, same data and same protocol — each axis's effect isolated and measured.
- **A clear, actionable recipe**: KosMos / Paligemma + continuous actions + Policy Head + OXE post-train is the current best practice for new VLA work, giving researchers a concrete starting point.
- **The open-source RoboVLMs framework**: a meta VLA builder that lets any VLM backbone and policy structure be mixed and matched. Code, models, datasets, toolkits, and training/evaluation recipes are all public at [robovlms.github.io](https://robovlms.github.io).
- **An emergent self-correction observation**: the best VLA (KosMos P.H.) produces self-correcting behavior absent from its training data — suggesting that the VLM's rich prior helps recover from imperfect policy attempts.

### Limitations

- **A snapshot guidebook**: this is a late-2024 picture of VLMs and VLAs. As PaliGemma successors, Gemini Robotics, or π₀.₅-scale models land, some conclusions will need re-verification.
- **Action representation limited to regression**: generative action heads (diffusion / flow matching, as in π₀ and Diffusion Policy) are outside the studied space — the additional design dimension inside the policy head is only partially covered in the appendix.
- **Single embodiment for real-robot evaluation**: the 20 tasks live on a single 7-DoF robot — how the VLM's grounding ability generalizes across embodiment variation still needs separate validation.
- **No data-efficiency curve for OXE post-train**: the paper establishes that post-train is best, but doesn't fully chart the trade-off curve of "when to stop OXE post-train so in-domain performance isn't lost."
