## TL;DR

This paper interprets part of RL's success in contact-rich manipulation as smoothing over contact modes, then transfers that idea into model-based global planning. It locally smooths quasi-dynamic contact models and uses the resulting metrics and extension steps to adapt RRT to contact-rich manipulation.

## Problem

Contact-rich manipulation is hard because the dynamics change abruptly whenever contacts are made, broken, or sliding. Enumerating contact modes leads to combinatorial explosion, while gradient-based trajectory optimization often gets stuck at non-smooth points or poor local minima. RL can average over these discontinuities through sampling, but it is computationally heavy and less explicit. This paper makes that smoothing effect explicit inside a contact model and a sampling-based planner.

## Core Idea

The core idea is to smooth contact dynamics into a local model that is more useful for planning than the raw non-smooth dynamics.

- **Smoothing view**: randomized smoothing and analytic smoothing are treated as two ways to build a local surrogate model.
- **Quasi-dynamic contact model**: frictional contact is relaxed into a convex differentiable quasi-dynamic formulation.
- **RRT integration**: the smoothed local model provides a reachability-aware Mahalanobis metric and contact-aware extension rule for tree growth.

<Collapsible title="Detailed Methods">

### Local smoothing

Instead of trusting a single gradient of a non-smooth function, the method builds a surrogate dynamics model by averaging over a local neighborhood. Randomized smoothing estimates this through samples, while analytic smoothing obtains derivatives from the relaxed contact model. The paper shows that both approaches produce closely related local linear models in simple systems and similar planning behavior in richer contact settings.

### Convex quasi-dynamic contact

The authors avoid full hybrid contact-mode enumeration. They formulate frictional contact with an Anitescu-style relaxation and log-barrier smoothing, producing a convex and differentiable quasi-dynamic contact model. This first-order model is not meant to perfectly reproduce all inertial effects; it is meant to guide planning toward reachable contact transitions.

### Enhanced RRT

Euclidean distance is a poor nearest-node metric in contact systems because equally distant states may differ greatly in reachability. The enhanced RRT uses the smoothed dynamics to define a local metric, samples contact-making states, and extends the tree using local contact-aware models.

</Collapsible>

## Key Results

- **Trajectory optimization case study**: analytic and randomized smoothing reduce costs more reliably than exact non-smoothed dynamics.
- **RRT case study**: contact sampling and local metrics substantially improve tree growth over vanilla/global-metric/no-contact-sampling ablations.
- **Task coverage**: the planner handles in-hand manipulation, plate pickup, door opening, pen placement, and bimanual bucket rotation.
- **Compute perspective**: it provides a model-based route to contact-rich behavior without large-scale RL policy training.

## Achievements and Limitations

**Key achievements**
- Translates RL's contact smoothing intuition into explicit model-based planning.
- Builds contact-rich RRT without enumerating contact modes.
- Compares analytic and randomized smoothing under a shared local-model view.

**Limitations**
- The quasi-dynamic first-order model can mismatch real second-order dynamics.
- Sim-to-real error is larger in 3D real systems than in simpler planar setups.
- Contact sampling still contains some non-physical teleportation assumptions.
- 🤖 The result is planner-centric; closed-loop tactile feedback and integration with modern policy/VLA systems remain open.
