## What I Wanted to Figure Out

- Where value moved when calculators and computers made knowledge work more efficient
- Whether the durable business was calculation labor, calculator education, or calculator sales
- What business zones open up when AI agents make coding and office automation cheaper
- Where the calculator analogy breaks when applied to AI agents
- Whether vertical SaaS remains enough when customers can copy internal tools with AI coding agents
- What an "outcome-responsible" business should look like between pure SaaS and pure outsourcing

## Lessons

- The important moment was not the invention of the calculator, but the point at which calculation entered business systems. The shift from Pascaline to Arithmometer, Comptometer, punch cards, mainframes, and spreadsheets was not just faster arithmetic. It was the redesign of accounting, payroll, insurance, inventory, and statistics as operating workflows.
- A person who simply owns a calculator and calculates faster for others can make money during the transition, but the position does not last. Once the tool spreads, calculation itself loses scarcity. Data collection, verification, reporting, and responsibility become the real bottlenecks.
- The AI agent era may follow a similar value migration: simple AI labor, then service bureaus, then workflow autopilots, then outcome businesses, then governance layers. The money attaches less to "using AI" and more to "the work is completed."
- AI agents are more dangerous and broader than calculators. Calculation is relatively deterministic. Agents can send emails, request payments, answer customers, deploy code, and manipulate external systems. That makes verification, auditability, permissions, governance, and liability far more expensive.
- Plain vertical SaaS may become weaker. As AI coding agents lower the cost of internal tools, customers may stop waiting for a vendor roadmap and rebuild systems around their own process reality.
- The durable form is neither "selling software" nor "being a pure agency." It looks more like managed agentic operations: a shared operating kernel, customer-specific adapters, a human exception desk, evals, audit logs, SLAs, and continuous improvement loops around a concrete business outcome.

## FAQ

### Q. When did calculators start changing society?

- Mechanical calculators trace back to the 17th-century Pascaline, but early machines were expensive and scarce, so their broad social impact was limited.
- In the late 19th century, commercial machines such as the Arithmometer and Comptometer mechanized repetitive office calculation in banks, accounting, insurance, and government.
- From the 1930s to the 1970s, punch cards, service bureaus, and mainframes expanded "calculation" into the data processing industry.
- In the 1970s, electronic handheld calculators sharply weakened the market for slide rules, mechanical calculators, and specialist calculation labor.
- In the 1980s, PCs and spreadsheets moved calculation power from specialist organizations onto the desk of individual knowledge workers.

### Q. Did people make big money by using calculators for customers who did not have them?

- For a short period, yes. When tools were expensive and access was scarce, the ability to calculate faster had market value.
- Over time, the position weakened. Calculators became cheaper, easier, and widely available, and customers learned to use the tools themselves.
- A calculation result alone has weak lock-in. Someone else can always perform the same calculation more cheaply.
- The stronger business was owning the workflow that contained calculation: payroll, accounting, billing, inventory, statistics, and insurance, where data entry, verification, reporting, and accountability were bundled.
- Training also made money during the transition, but tool usage eventually moved into products and standard operating procedures.

### Q. Where does money appear in the AI agent era?

- The first priority is the vertical outcome agent: customer support, insurance claims, revenue cycle management, accounting close, legal review, procurement, and other work with relatively clear completion units.
- The second is AI-native BPO or managed agent operations. Instead of growing revenue by adding people, these businesses use agent throughput, evals, human review, and SLAs as leverage.
- The third is the agent control plane: permissions, audit logs, eval suites, hallucination guardrails, human approval routing, cost controls, and incident response.
- The fourth is workflow data, process mining, and enterprise context. Agents need to understand the company's real process flow and exception rules before they can work.
- The fifth is the AI software factory and legacy modernization: replacing old internal tools, Excel macros, Access databases, and manual reports with agents and internal apps.
- Education can make money, but it is not the largest zone. Strong education is not "how to use ChatGPT"; it is workflow redesign, governance, SOP design, and rollout.

### Q. Where does the calculator analogy break?

- Calculator outputs are comparatively easy to verify. Agent outputs involve documents, customer replies, code changes, procurement judgments, and other context-heavy actions.
- Calculators were tools that users operated directly. Agents increasingly look like delegated executors.
- Calculator adoption often required buying a physical device. AI agents can be absorbed into existing SaaS, ERP, CRM, and IDE products.
- The service bureau window may be shorter. Mainframes were expensive and scarce; AI agents are cloud software, so customers can experiment quickly.
- Defensibility therefore comes less from model usage and more from data, workflow ownership, integration, permission design, auditability, and responsibility for outcomes.

### Q. Why might vertical SaaS be weaker now?

- As AI coding agents improve, customers can look at an external SaaS product and build a similar internal tool around their own process.
- A customer may love the first version, then discover mismatches with its ERP, approval rules, supplier master, and exception handling.
- In the Lotus era, buying software was the natural path. In the AI coding era, understanding the feature may be enough to rebuild it internally.
- Screens, forms, basic workflows, simple reports, and prompt wrappers face high copy pressure.
- The defensibility of vertical SaaS must therefore come from repeatedly completing the business outcome better, not from having the feature first.

### Q. What should an outcome-responsible business look like?

- Pure SaaS is too hands-off, and pure BPO is not scalable when each customer requires a separate operations team.
- The middle form has a standardized agent operating kernel, while customer-specific process differences are absorbed through adapters and configuration.
- Agents handle normal cases, a human exception desk handles risky or unusual cases, and every action leaves audit logs and eval traces.
- Pricing moves from seats to units such as throughput, completed work, savings, quality SLA, and reduced risk.
- In procurement, the product is not "procurement automation software." It is supplier communication, quote comparison, purchase order generation, approval, delivery risk tracking, and compliance completed as an operating result.
- The core asset is not code. It is workflow data, exception records, domain ontology, eval sets, integrations, trust, and a history of taking responsibility.

### Q. Which work fits managed agentic operations?

- Good work has high repetition, verifiable outcomes, recurring exceptions, and customer differences that can be expressed as parameters.
- Error cost matters. Customers pay when payroll, compliance, delivery, quality, billing, or contracts can fail in costly ways.
- Existing outsourcing or hidden labor makes the budget easier to replace. The business substitutes an existing cost rather than asking for a new budget category.
- Work connected to systems of record is better. The agent becomes an operating layer over ERP, MES, QMS, LIMS, CRM, or similar systems, not a shallow wrapper.
- Bad work is fully custom per customer, has ambiguous outcomes, happens once, or belongs to a customer's core strategic differentiation and will be pulled in-house.
- Work that is only UI convenience, or where all learning stays inside one customer, gives the outside vendor a weak moat.
